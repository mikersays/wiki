# Scientists Detect Signature of Life on Exoplanet K2-18b Using Webb Telescope
**Source:** https://www.cnn.com/2025/04/17/science/k218b-potential-biosignature-webb
**Author:** Ashley Strickland, CNN
**Date:** April 17, 2025

## Summary

Astronomers led by **Nikku Madhusudhan** at the **University of Cambridge** have detected chemical fingerprints suggesting the presence of **dimethyl sulfide (DMS)** and potentially **dimethyl disulfide (DMDS)** in the atmosphere of the exoplanet **K2-18b**, using the **James Webb Space Telescope's Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI)**. On Earth, both molecules are exclusively produced by microbial life, typically marine phytoplankton, making this the strongest potential biosignature ever detected on an exoplanet. The study was published in **The Astrophysical Journal Letters** on April 17, 2025.

## About K2-18b

K2-18b is located **124 light-years from Earth** and is classified as potentially a **"Hycean world"** -- a habitable planet entirely covered in liquid water with a hydrogen-rich atmosphere. The planet has **8.6 times Earth's mass** and **2.6 times Earth's diameter**, and orbits within its star's habitable zone, where conditions allow liquid water to exist on its surface. The research team, led by Madhusudhan, first theorized the concept of Hycean worlds in 2021 and had previously detected liquid water oceans on K2-18b.

## Detection Method and Instruments

The team used multiple Webb instruments across different observation campaigns:

- **Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph (NIRISS)** and **Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec)**: Previously detected methane and carbon dioxide in K2-18b's atmosphere.
- **Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI)**: Used for the new DMS/DMDS detection, analyzing a different wavelength range of light with no overlap with previous observations.

The detection method involved analyzing light passing through K2-18b's atmosphere as the planet transited in front of its host star, revealing chemical signatures of atmospheric gases through transmission spectroscopy.

Madhusudhan stated: "This is an independent line of evidence, using a different instrument than we did before and a different wavelength range of light, where there is no overlap with the previous observations. The signal came through strong and clear."

## Statistical Significance and Caveats

The detection has reached a **three-sigma significance level**, meaning there is only a **0.3% probability** that the detections occurred by chance. However, a definitive scientific discovery requires a **five-sigma threshold** (below 0.00006% chance of random occurrence). The study authors acknowledge that more data is needed.

Key limitations include:

- DMS and DMDS concentrations are estimated at **thousands of times stronger than Earth levels**, where they typically remain below one part per billion. This raises questions about the plausibility of biological production at such scales.
- The molecules could potentially be produced through unknown non-biological chemical processes on the planet.
- The absence of **ethane (C2H6)**, which ultraviolet rays should theoretically produce if DMS/DMDS breakdown occurred, suggests atmospheric models may be incomplete.

Madhusudhan cautioned: "It's important that we're deeply skeptical of our own results, because it's only by testing and testing again that we will be able to reach the point where we're confident in them."

## Scientific Community Response

The findings generated significant interest and debate across the astronomical community:

**Supportive views:**
- **Eddie Schwieterman** (UC Riverside, astrobiology) called himself "somewhat skeptical but very intrigued and excited about what's next." His own research found that sustaining such DMS abundance would require production rates "about 20 times that of Earth's DMS production... a high bar, but plausible."
- **Dr. David Clements** (Imperial College London) deemed it "a step in the right direction" despite not meeting discovery thresholds.

**Skeptical views:**
- **Sara Seager** (MIT astrophysicist, not involved in the research) noted that independent teams interpret K2-18b differently -- some proposing a Hycean world, others suggesting a "hot magma ocean" or "mini-Neptune." She warned: "enthusiasm is outpacing evidence" and predicted the finding "will remain in the candidate category indefinitely."
- A separate analysis (reported by NPR, April 25, 2025) of the same data cast doubt on the earlier findings, with what was described as an "overwhelming consensus" that the enthusiasm expressed in making the announcement far exceeded the strength of the evidence.

## Next Steps

- An additional **16-24 hours** of Webb observation time could enable definitive evidence claims, according to the researchers.
- Data will be released for independent validation by other astronomical teams.
- Confirmation requires higher statistical significance and validation from multiple independent groups.
- Future observations of similarly sized exoplanets in habitable zones would strengthen or contextualize these findings.

Madhusudhan posed a broader philosophical question: "A bigger question in my mind is whether we as a species are prepared to find life as we don't know it."

## Broader Context

This discovery builds on JWST's broader campaign to characterize exoplanet atmospheres. The telescope has been systematically studying potentially habitable exoplanets since its science operations began in 2022. The K2-18b observations represent one of the most ambitious attempts to detect biosignature gases beyond the solar system and illustrate both the power and limitations of current detection capabilities. The scientific community remains divided on whether K2-18b is truly a Hycean world capable of supporting life, or a very different type of planet where non-biological processes could explain the observed chemical signatures.
