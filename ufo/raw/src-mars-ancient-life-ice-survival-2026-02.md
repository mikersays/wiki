# NASA study finds ancient life could survive 50 million years in Martian ice

Mars' frozen ice caps may serve as time capsules for ancient life. Laboratory experiments conducted by researchers from NASA Goddard Space Flight Center and Penn State demonstrated that amino acids—key protein building blocks—can survive tens of millions of years when preserved in pure ice, even under continuous cosmic radiation. However, ice mixed with Martian-like soil destroys organic material far more quickly. These findings suggest future missions should prioritize drilling into clean, buried ice rather than studying rocks or dirt.

Scientists recreated Martian conditions in the laboratory to test whether ancient microbes or their traces could persist in Martian permafrost. They found that amino acids from E. coli bacteria, if trapped in Martian ice caps, could survive more than 50 million years despite constant cosmic radiation exposure. The research was published in Astrobiology and indicates that Mars exploration missions should focus on pure ice or ice-rich permafrost instead of primarily investigating rocks, clay, or soil.

The study was led by Alexander Pavlov, a space scientist at NASA Goddard. The research team sealed E. coli bacteria inside test tubes filled with pure water ice, with other samples combined with water and materials commonly found in Martian sediment, including silicate-based rocks and clay. These frozen samples were placed in a gamma radiation chamber at Penn State's Radiation Science and Engineering Center, cooled to minus 60 degrees Fahrenheit to match Martian icy regions. The bacteria were exposed to radiation equivalent to 20 million years of cosmic ray bombardment on the Martian surface. Samples were then vacuum sealed and shipped to NASA Goddard under cold conditions for amino acid testing, with researchers modeling an additional 30 years of radiation exposure to reach the 50 million-year total.

Results proved striking: more than 10 percent of amino acids survived the full 50 million-year simulation in pure water ice. By contrast, samples mixed with Mars-like sediment broke down 10 times faster and did not survive. A 2022 study by the same team showed that amino acids preserved in a mixture of 10 percent water ice and 90 percent Martian soil were destroyed more rapidly than samples containing only sediment.

Researchers theorize that faster breakdown in mixed samples occurs because a thin film forms where ice contacts minerals. This layer could allow radiation to move more freely and damage amino acids. Conversely, in solid ice, harmful particles created by radiation become frozen in place and may be unable to reach organic compounds.

The team also tested organic material at temperatures similar to those on Europa, Jupiter's icy moon, and Enceladus, Saturn's icy moon. At these even colder temperatures, deterioration slowed further. These findings are encouraging for NASA's Europa Clipper mission, which will study Europa's ice shell and subsurface ocean. Europa Clipper launched in 2024 and is traveling 1.8 billion miles to reach Jupiter in 2030, where it will perform 49 close flybys to determine whether subsurface environments could support life.

Accessing buried ice on Mars will require appropriate tools. The 2008 NASA Mars Phoenix mission was the first to dig down and photograph ice in the Martian equivalent of the Arctic Circle. Most Martian ice lies just below the surface, requiring future missions to have a large enough drill or powerful scoop similar to Phoenix's design and capabilities.

The research team included Christopher House (Penn State professor of geosciences), Zhidan Zhang (retired lab technologist), and NASA Goddard scientists Hannah McLain, Kendra Farnsworth, Daniel Glavin, Jamie Elsila, and Jason Dworkin. NASA's Planetary Science Division funded the work.
