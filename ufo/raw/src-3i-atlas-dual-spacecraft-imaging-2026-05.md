# Dual spacecraft capture both hemispheres of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at once

The Southwest Research Institute-led Ultraviolet Spectrograph (UVS) instruments aboard ESA's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) spacecraft and NASA's Europa Clipper made unique observations of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS in late 2025. SwRI leads the UVS instruments on both spacecraft, simultaneously imaging both hemispheres of the comet and detecting ultraviolet emissions.

Only the third recognized interstellar object, 3I/ATLAS entered our solar system in July 2025. As the comet passed between the two spacecraft in November 2025, researchers informally coordinated observations.

"We observed hydrogen, oxygen and carbon emissions," explained Dr. Kurt Retherford, principal investigator. "These elements are produced when gases escaping the comet's nucleus break apart when exposed to sunlight."

The observations provided rare simultaneous views of the comet's coma from two directions. Europa Clipper captured the night side showing scattered dust, while Juice imaged glowing gas on the day side, with elements extending over 5 million kilometers from the nucleus.

"This was the first time we've had simultaneous direct views of a comet's coma from two directions."

Researchers found elevated carbon emissions compared to typical solar system comets, corroborating findings about the comet's interstellar origin. By studying water ice and dry ice ratios, scientists can compare this interstellar comet's composition to native solar system comets, revealing whether its home solar system resembles ours.

"This helps us understand if the solar system where 3I/ATLAS formed is similar to ours or different."
