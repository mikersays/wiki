# More than a decade after its disappearance, Malaysia to resume search for MH370

A Texas-based company is restarting the search for Malaysian flight MH370 today. The plane was on a routine flight from Kuala Lumpur to Beijing more than 11 years ago when it vanished with 239 people on board.

Flight MH370 took off from Kuala Lumpur after midnight in March 2014. Less than an hour into the flight, the plane disappeared. Many hours passed before American passenger Philip Wood's partner, Sarah Bajc, heard anything from Malaysian authorities.

Bajc recalled the initial response: "we're not sure. We'll call you back."

For years, families of those on board have waited for accurate news about their loved ones. Malaysia's government says it is committed to finding the Boeing 777 and has tasked the marine robotics company Ocean Infinity to resume the search in the southern Indian Ocean.

According to Professor Charitha Pattiaratchi at the University of Western Australia, "They're going to look at an area of about 10,000 square miles" — roughly the size of Tasmania or Sri Lanka. He notes that plane parts could still be discovered given that conditions at the ocean floor remain relatively stable.

Pattiaratchi explained the search challenge involves looking for objects comparable to "a bus" from extreme distances while navigating an unseen underwater environment. However, he acknowledges that technology has improved significantly since the plane's disappearance.

If Ocean Infinity locates the wreckage, the company will receive $70 million in payment. For the passengers' families, discovering the aircraft could finally provide answers about what caused the plane to vanish.

Bajc, whose partner remains missing, described the ongoing uncertainty: "The ambiguity of really not knowing still is still painful." This latest search effort offers families renewed hope after nearly 12 years of waiting.
