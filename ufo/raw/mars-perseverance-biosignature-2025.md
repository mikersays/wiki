# NASA Says Mars Rover Discovered Potential Biosignature in Cheyava Falls Rock
**Source:** https://www.jpl.nasa.gov/news/nasa-says-mars-rover-discovered-potential-biosignature-last-year/
**Author:** NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory
**Date:** September 10, 2025

## Summary

NASA's **Perseverance rover** collected a rock sample called **"Sapphire Canyon"** from a formation nicknamed **"Cheyava Falls"** in **July 2024**. After more than a year of intensive analysis, researchers determined the sample "contains potential biosignatures," according to findings published in the journal **Nature** in September 2025. This represents what NASA described as the "clearest sign yet" of possible ancient microbial life on Mars, though scientists emphasize the findings are not definitive proof and require further validation.

## Location and Geological Context

The discovery occurred in the **Bright Angel formation** within **Neretva Vallis**, an ancient river valley roughly **400 meters wide** that was carved by water flowing into **Jezero Crater** billions of years ago. Jezero Crater, Perseverance's landing site since **February 2021**, was selected precisely because it shows clear evidence of having once contained a lake fed by river channels.

The sedimentary rocks in the Bright Angel formation comprise **clay and silt** -- materials that are excellent preservers of microbial life on Earth. These rocks are rich in:

- **Organic carbon**
- **Sulfur**
- **Oxidized iron (rust)**
- **Phosphorus**

These elements are all considered essential ingredients for life as we know it and provide potential energy sources for microbial metabolism.

## Key Minerals and Chemical Signatures

Scientists identified two iron-rich minerals arranged in distinctive patterns called **"leopard spots"** -- small nodules and "reaction fronts" that are visually and chemically similar to structures created by microbial activity in terrestrial sediments:

- **Vivianite** (hydrated iron phosphate, also known as ferrous iron phosphate): This mineral is frequently found in Earth sediments around decaying organic matter. Its presence suggests the rock interacted with organic material in a water-rich, low-temperature environment.
- **Greigite** (iron sulfide): This mineral is notably produced by certain Earth microbes (iron-reducing and sulfate-reducing bacteria). On Earth, greigite formation is strongly associated with microbial metabolisms.

These minerals appeared to form through **electron-transfer (redox) reactions** between sediment and organic matter, potentially representing what lead researcher **Joel Hurowitz** (Stony Brook University) described as a "fingerprint for microbial life."

Hurowitz noted that "the combination of chemical compounds... could have been a rich source of energy for microbial metabolisms."

## Scientific Instruments Used

Two key instruments aboard Perseverance were used for the analysis:

- **PIXL** (Planetary Instrument for X-ray Lithochemistry): An X-ray fluorescence spectrometer that maps the chemical composition of rock surfaces at fine scales. PIXL was responsible for identifying the elemental distribution patterns in the "leopard spot" formations.
- **SHERLOC** (Scanning Habitable Environments with Raman & Luminescence for Organics & Chemicals): A spectrometer that uses ultraviolet laser light to detect organic molecules and mineral compositions. SHERLOC confirmed the presence of organic molecules in the Bright Angel formation rocks.

## Important Caveats

Scientists emphasize several important limitations of these findings:

1. **"Organic" does not necessarily mean biological**: The organic matter detected could have been produced by abiotic (non-living) processes, such as volcanic activity, meteorite delivery, or chemical reactions with water.
2. **Mineral formation alternatives**: Vivianite and greigite can form abiotically through sustained high temperatures, acidic conditions, or organic compound binding without biological involvement.
3. **However**: The Bright Angel rocks show **no evidence of such extreme conditions** (high temperatures or strong acidity), which makes the abiotic explanations less likely in this specific geological context.
4. **A potential biosignature** is defined as a substance or structure that might have a biological origin but requires more data or further study before a conclusion about the presence or absence of life can be reached.

**Katie Stack Morgan**, Perseverance's project scientist at JPL, stressed that "astrobiological claims... require extraordinary evidence" and that peer-review publication in Nature represents a crucial but not final validation step.

## Broader Implications

The discovery is particularly significant for several reasons:

- The Bright Angel formation contains **younger sedimentary rocks** than previously expected to preserve evidence of life, suggesting Mars may have **remained habitable for longer** than previously theorized.
- The combination of minerals, organic carbon, and evidence of past water activity creates a compelling (though not conclusive) case for ancient biological processes.
- Sapphire Canyon represents one of **27 rock cores** collected by Perseverance since its landing. These samples are intended for eventual return to Earth by a future **Mars Sample Return** mission, where they could be analyzed with far more sophisticated laboratory equipment.

## What Comes Next

The definitive answer about whether these signatures represent ancient Martian life will likely require the physical return of the Sapphire Canyon sample to Earth. Only terrestrial laboratories can perform the kind of detailed isotopic analysis, electron microscopy, and other sophisticated tests needed to distinguish biological from abiotic origins. The Mars Sample Return mission, though facing budget and timeline challenges, remains the best hope for resolving this question.
