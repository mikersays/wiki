# Congress Wants to Know More About the Military's UAP Intercepts Around North America
**Source:** https://defensescoop.com/2025/12/10/uap-ufo-military-intercepts-north-america-fy-2026-ndaa/
**Author:** DefenseScoop staff
**Date:** December 10, 2025

## Overview

This DefenseScoop article details the UAP-related provisions included in the fiscal year 2026 National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), the primary legislative vehicle for U.S. defense policy. The provisions represent the most concrete legislative actions taken to date to force Pentagon transparency on UAP intercept operations, classification practices, and reporting structures. The article reflects the growing bipartisan Congressional consensus that the Department of Defense has not been sufficiently forthcoming about UAP encounters involving U.S. military assets.

## Three Key NDAA Provisions

### 1. Mandatory Briefings on North America UAP Intercepts

The most significant provision requires the AARO director to brief lawmakers on all UAP intercept operations conducted since 2004 by two specific commands:

- **NORAD** (North American Aerospace Defense Command): The binational U.S.-Canadian command responsible for aerospace warning and control for North America
- **U.S. Northern Command (USNORTHCOM)**: The unified combatant command responsible for homeland defense

The briefings must detail:
- The **number** of UAP intercepts
- The **location** of each intercept
- The **nature** of the phenomena encountered
- The **procedures and protocols** followed during intercept operations
- The **data collected** during operations

The 2004 start date is significant because it encompasses the famous USS Nimitz "Tic Tac" encounter of November 2004, which became one of the foundational incidents of the modern UAP era when it was publicly revealed in 2017. By requiring a comprehensive accounting from 2004 forward, Congress is effectively demanding a complete inventory of every UAP intercept conducted by the commands responsible for defending North American airspace over a 20+ year period.

### 2. Classification Review and Transparency

The legislation directs AARO to account for all security classification guides used in UAP reports and investigations. Jordan Flowers of the Disclosure Foundation explained the significance: this provision "responds to long-standing concerns about **overclassification** and improper restrictions on the release of imagery or data."

The overclassification issue has been a persistent complaint from UAP transparency advocates, who argue that legitimate UAP evidence is being classified not because its release would compromise sources and methods, but because disclosure would be embarrassing or politically inconvenient. By requiring AARO to document and justify its classification practices, Congress is attempting to create accountability for classification decisions.

This provision also responds to specific allegations from whistleblowers and former government officials who have claimed that UAP imagery and sensor data are classified at levels that prevent even members of Congress with appropriate clearances from accessing them.

### 3. Streamlined Reporting and Data Consolidation

A third provision eliminates duplicative reporting requirements across federal agencies and consolidates data submission processes to AARO. Prior to this change, multiple agencies maintained separate reporting channels and databases, creating fragmentation that made comprehensive analysis difficult. The consolidation aims to ensure that AARO has a complete picture of UAP encounters across all domains -- air, sea, space, and ground.

## Background and Context

### AARO's Current Status

The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office was officially launched in 2022 under the Biden administration as the successor to earlier UAP investigation efforts (the UAP Task Force and before that, AATIP). By the time of this article's publication, AARO had investigated over 650 cases. The office operates under the Department of Defense and reports to both the Secretary of Defense and the Director of National Intelligence.

### Driving Factors

The NDAA provisions were driven by several converging factors:

- **Increasing UAP reports**: The volume of reports from military personnel, commercial pilots, and civilians continued to rise throughout 2025
- **Drone/UAP incursions**: Unexplained drone and UAP incursions near military installations and critical infrastructure raised safety and security concerns
- **Transparency advocacy**: Organizations like the Disclosure Foundation and Americans for Safe Aerospace lobbied for legislative action
- **Whistleblower testimony**: Ongoing testimony from former government and military personnel claiming knowledge of programs and encounters not disclosed to Congress

### Legislative Process

The provisions were included in the conferenced (final reconciled) version of the fiscal 2026 NDAA, meaning both the House and Senate agreed to their inclusion. However, the article notes that the legislation required presidential signature before becoming law.

## Significance

The NDAA provisions represent a qualitative escalation in Congressional oversight of UAP programs. While previous legislation (such as the 2024 NDAA) established AARO and mandated general reporting, the FY2026 provisions are more specific and demanding:

- They target specific military commands (NORAD, USNORTHCOM) rather than the Pentagon in general
- They specify a time period (since 2004) rather than leaving the scope open-ended
- They require detailed operational information (number, location, nature, procedures, data) rather than summary reports
- They address the classification system itself, not just the classified information

These provisions reflect Congressional frustration with what lawmakers have described as a "lack of transparency" from Pentagon officials regarding UAP encounters. The specificity of the requirements suggests that Congress has reason to believe significant UAP intercept data exists but has not been shared with legislators.

## Key Organizations Referenced

- **AARO** (All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office): Pentagon's primary UAP investigation body
- **NORAD**: Binational U.S.-Canadian aerospace defense command
