# Lue Elizondo: UFO Files Contradict NASA's Longstanding Claims

The White House's UFO file release officially kicked off May 8; however, "believers" and lawmakers say the biggest bombshells are still hidden behind closed doors.

Earlier this month, the Trump administration launched a new federal portal dedicated to declassified UFO and UAP material, releasing more than 150 documents, military encounter reports, cockpit videos and intelligence files tied to unexplained aerial phenomena. While technically, there wasn't any new information included, it signaled a step in the right direction in terms of transparency.

One prominent voice in the UFO transparency movement, Lue Elizondo, said the recent disclosure effort may not have delivered the "explosive revelations" many expected, but argued the release still marked a major turning point; particularly regarding NASA's long-denied possession of historical UAP material connected to the Apollo missions. He pointed specifically to newly released lunar photographs and audio recordings, saying they appear to contradict decades of public statements from NASA claiming it held no significant UAP-related evidence in its archives.

NASA's own UAP FAQ page and its 2023 UAP report rollout, read, "NASA does not actively search for UAP." NASA Administrator Bill Nelson also stated in 2023, "The NASA independent study team did not find any evidence that UAP have an extraterrestrial origin...but we don't know what these UAP are" while acknowledging the "global fascination." The UFO files did, in fact, include UAP evidence in the NASA-labeled filings. Again, from audio recordings to testimony regarding UAP sightings from pilots and scientists in a UFO and Defense Report.

Elizondo, Former Director of AATIP, Disclosure and Transparency Advocate and author, told Los Angeles, "I was happy to see the Administration take this topic seriously and begin the process of increasing public transparency. Although some of the information may seem less than what was expected, of particular significance were the lunar photos taken by NASA during the Apollo missions. For decades NASA has said it had nothing related to UAP in its holdings and we now know that was not accurate. Between photographs and audio recordings, it's apparent that NASA does have some historical information regarding UAP."

The White House has called the rollout an "unprecedented step toward transparency." But online speculation has now exploded over whether a much larger "Phase 2" disclosure is coming next.

"There's definitely more coming," Rep. Anna Paulina Luna previously indicated while pushing for the release of dozens of classified military UAP videos requested earlier this year by Congress. Those 46 videos, believed to include military encounters and infrared footage, were notably absent from the first batch of records released to the public. Luna had requested these videos from the Department of War's Pete Hegseth in March, with an April 14 deadline. The deadline was missed due to an "administrative error," and there has been no indication of when the videos will surface.

The initial disclosure dump included pilot sightings, Apollo-era transcripts, military reports describing strange "orb" activity, FBI memoranda and previously unseen footage involving unidentified objects near Japan and over the Pacific.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said the administration wants Americans to have "unprecedented transparency" into decades of government-held UAP material, while White House officials insisted the public can now "make up their own minds."

Still, UFO researchers and online sleuths quickly noticed many of the released documents had already circulated in fragments for years within the UFO community, claiming that the first rollout was more symbolic than revelatory.

The White House has not officially confirmed a "Phase 2" timeline, but lawmakers involved in the disclosure effort have suggested additional releases could occur within weeks. But does the President have the ability to release the classified information that could change the world? Elizondo isn't so sure.

He suggested that any resistance to larger UAP disclosure efforts may be happening behind the scenes within the intelligence community; internal opposition could be slowing or obstructing the administration's push for transparency despite public promises from the White House.

"As for the greater effort to release information, it appears elements within our intelligence community are very much resisting the President's order and undermining the DNI. I remain hopeful; however, that the President's will can overcome these roadblocks," said Elizondo.

For now, the mystery appears far from over.
