# Transparency, Safety and Science: The UAP Landscape in 2025
**Source:** https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/year-in-review/transparency-safety-and-science-the-uap-landscape-in-2025/
**Author:** Aerospace America / AIAA (American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics)
**Date:** 2025 (year-in-review)

## Overview

This Aerospace America year-in-review article provides the most authoritative professional engineering society perspective on how UAP research matured in 2025. Published by AIAA, the world's largest technical society dedicated to aerospace, it documents the month-by-month institutionalization of UAP investigation across government, academia, and international bodies. The article frames the shift as one from stigma and ad hoc reporting to structured scientific inquiry and policy infrastructure.

## January 2025: Academic Breakthrough

A peer-reviewed paper titled "The New Science of Unidentified Aerospace-Undersea Phenomena (UAP)" was published on arXiv, consolidating decades of government research and establishing a formal framework for scientific investigation. This milestone marked UAP research's transition from the margins of speculation into mainstream academic discourse. The paper provided a taxonomy and methodology that subsequent researchers could build upon, making it a foundational document for the emerging field.

## February 2025: Congressional and Pentagon Reforms

Congress advanced legislative transparency measures targeting UAP disclosure. Simultaneously, the Pentagon's All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) implemented significant reforms to professionalize case tracking and data management. The reforms aimed to replace informal, ad hoc practices from earlier years with structured collection processes and formalized declassification pathways. This represented a shift from reactive investigation to proactive institutional infrastructure.

## April 2025: Rice University InterfaceRice Conference

Rice University hosted the InterfaceRice conference, which brought together engineers, social scientists, historians, and defense experts to discuss UAP policy standards and research priorities. The conference demonstrated growing academic legitimacy for UAP studies, with participants from diverse disciplines engaging seriously with the topic. This cross-disciplinary approach was notable for treating UAP not merely as a defense or intelligence issue but as a broader scientific and societal challenge.

## May 2025: AARO Case-Management Architecture

AARO developed a new case-management architecture that integrated inputs from multiple sources: the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), civil aviation operators, and military sensor systems. This architecture reframed anomalous reports primarily as **safety-of-flight concerns** rather than purely intelligence or defense matters. The safety framing was strategically significant because it provided a non-stigmatized rationale for reporting and investigation, potentially increasing the flow of data from commercial aviation sources.

## July 2025: Canada's Sky Canada Project

Canada's Office of the Chief Science Advisor released the Sky Canada Project report, which became the most comprehensive national UAP framework outside the United States. The report's key recommendations included:

- Establishment of a dedicated UAP office under the Canadian Space Agency
- Development of a bilingual (English/French) public reporting app
- Creation of a standardized database for UAP reports

This represented the first time a major U.S. ally produced a formal, government-backed framework for systematic UAP investigation, signaling that the issue was gaining international traction beyond the United States.

## September 2025: House Oversight Committee Hearing

The House Oversight Committee held a hearing titled "Restoring Public Trust Through UAP Transparency and Whistleblower Protection." Key developments included:

- **Military testimony**: Former Military Police Officer Jeffrey Nuccetelli and Dylan Borland provided direct testimony about their UAP encounters during military service
- **Legislative action**: Representative Robert Garcia reintroduced H.R. 5231, the Safe Airspace for Americans Act, which received endorsement from AIAA
- **Whistleblower protection**: The UAP Whistleblower Protection Act was introduced to protect government and military personnel who report UAP findings, data, or funding information

The AIAA endorsement of the Safe Airspace for Americans Act was particularly significant, as it represented the aerospace engineering establishment formally backing UAP-related legislation.

## October 2025: FAA Terminology and Reporting Overhaul

The Federal Aviation Administration issued Notice JO 7110.800, which made two critical changes:

1. **Terminology**: Officially replaced the term "UFO" with "UAP" in all FAA communications and protocols
2. **Mandatory reporting**: Established new mandatory reporting protocols for air traffic control personnel encountering UAP, explicitly linking these reports to AARO frameworks

The notice framed UAP reporting as a potential **national security concern**, elevating its priority within the aviation safety infrastructure. This was a landmark bureaucratic change that formalized UAP as a recognized category within the world's largest air traffic control system.

## November 2025: NDAA Negotiations

UAP-related provisions remained under active negotiation in the National Defense Authorization Act for fiscal year 2026. The inclusion of UAP provisions in the NDAA -- the primary vehicle for U.S. defense policy -- signaled the **institutionalization** of UAP investigation within core defense planning processes. This represented a shift from UAP being an add-on or special interest item to being treated as a standard component of national defense legislation.

## Key Figures and Organizations

- **Ryan Graves**: Former Navy pilot and chair of the AIAA Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena Integration Committee, who described "highly credible people and professional observers" seeing objects with unexplained capabilities
- **Robert Powell**: Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies, who estimated $10-100 million would be needed for a network of 930 automated camera systems across the U.S.
- **AARO**: Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office, the primary U.S. government investigative body
- **AIAA**: American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics, providing professional engineering society legitimacy
- **University of Wurzburg**: Developing "AllSkyCAM" systems in collaboration with Germany's civil aviation authority
- **Galileo Project (Harvard)**: Designing sensor arrays to detect anomalies potentially of non-terrestrial origin

## Significance

The article's most important contribution is documenting how 2025 was the year UAP investigation became institutionalized across multiple sectors -- military, aviation safety, academia, and international government. The transition from stigmatized fringe topic to structured scientific and policy domain represents a fundamental shift in how the phenomenon is treated by establishment institutions.
