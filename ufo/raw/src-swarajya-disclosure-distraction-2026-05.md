# Disclosure Or Deceptive Distraction: Pentagon's Latest UAP Files Reveal Nothing New

On 8 May 2026, the Pentagon, now renamed as the 'War Department', finally claimed that it had surrendered its secrets to the digital winds.

Under the mandate of the Trump administration's PURSUE directive, the first major cache of declassified UAP files was uploaded to war.gov/ufo, a stark assembly of 162 documents and 28 sensor-driven vignettes totalling 41 minutes of infrared ambiguity.

While the Trump administration touted a new era of 'historic transparency' and the press heralded a definitive milestone in the long march toward disclosure, the digital sphere ignited with a more ancient fervour. For the true believers, these grainy frames were not merely data points in a bureaucratic ledger; they were the first blurred silhouettes of a non-human reality we are only just beginning to name.

It was none of those things.

In reality, the public was handed a polished collection of the same grainy, indistinct shapes that have characterised every official report for years. Much like the famous Navy footage released in 2020, these videos come from a single camera source and offer little clarity, leaving us with blurry smudges that are nearly impossible to identify with any certainty.

Grainy infrared specks described in the files themselves as "football-shaped bodies", "misshapen and uneven balls of white light", "eight-pointed stars", "oval-shaped objects", and "areas of contrast".

Distant orbs weaving near offshore wind turbines or skimming oceans at leisurely speeds while executing apparent '90-degree turns'.

An "area of contrast" in 2013 Middle East footage that looks suspiciously like classic bokeh and diffraction artefacts from an out-of-focus point source.

A 2023 Syria orb explicitly flagged in the documentation as a "light/glare halo effect".

A 2023 Greece sequence of an object making sharp manoeuvres at around 80 mph, precisely the kind of illusion produced by camera panning, parallax, and sensor motion. Analysts like Mick West at Metabunk have already begun the familiar work of demonstrating how these effects replicate earlier debunked cases.

A 2024 Indo-Pacific clip of a "football-shaped body" with radial projections captured on a single infrared sensor at distance.

Even the historical garnish — an Apollo-era moon flash, a 1965 Gemini audio clip of astronaut Frank Borman reporting an unidentified object — adds archival colour but zero empirical weight. These are the UFO community's greatest hits, dusted off and reissued under fresh classification stamps.

The novelty here is purely bureaucratic: these particular files, spanning 2013–2026 and drawn from operations in Iraq, Syria, the UAE, Greece, and the Indo-Pacific, had not previously leaked or been dribbled out in prior tranches.

The substance, however, is drearily familiar.

Every clip is short, low-fidelity, single-sensor data lacking multi-angle corroboration, full telemetry, or contextual clarity. The government's own summaries concede the obvious: these cases remain 'unresolved' not because they defy physics or show intelligence controlling their movement but because the evidence is too thin to resolve them at all.

AARO, the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office, has said as much in every public assessment. Most incidents that receive decent data eventually resolve to drones, balloons, aircraft, birds, flares, or optical and sensor artefacts.

This is not deception by fabrication. It is deception by hype.

The Pentagon earns modest credit for posting the raw files with minimal redaction; genuine openness on old records is a step forward. The problem lies in the marketing.

By bundling unresolved sensor glitches under the banner of 'UAP disclosures' and inviting the public to treat them as potential evidence of exotic technology or non-human intelligence, the exercise sustains a narrative that extraordinary claims have finally met extraordinary evidence.

They have not.

Then, there are critics who have gone further, suggesting that the timing of this release may serve as a strategic diversion. As reports of a disastrous and costly war with Iran continue to mount and pressure grows over the still-unreleased Epstein files, the spectacle of grainy UAP videos provides a convenient distraction for the public. Instead of sustained scrutiny on geopolitical failure or the accountability of the powerful, attention shifts to speculative lights in the sky. At least we can be sure that the aliens have not visited the island of sin of the filthy rich and powerful whom the present administration may be striving to protect.

The physical-evidence treadmill has been running in place for decades. Extraordinary claims — crash retrievals, reverse-engineered alien tech, 'non-human biologics', alien interviews, alien autopsy videos alleged in congressional testimony and pop-UFO culture for years — still lack the extraordinary proof required by any rigorous standard.

AARO's historical reviews have found no credible sign of hidden programmes or extraterrestrial hardware. The universe is vast enough that genuine anomalies deserve open investigation. But treating these latest infrared specks as paradigm-shifting revelations is spectacle, not epistemology.

The disclosures keep disappointing because they are looking in the wrong place.

The real mystery is not physical. It is psychological.

UFOs, now rebranded as UAPs, along with the far more intimate and disturbing 'abduction experiences' that often accompany them, are best understood as phenomena of the human psyche. They erupt at the intersection of altered states, cultural expectation, trauma, and also social upheavals. They possibly arise from a deeper layer of consciousness that our current materialist frameworks struggle to accommodate.

There is no compelling physical mystery left once the blurry dots are set aside.

The deeper puzzle is experiential: why do these encounters grip millions so powerfully? What do they do to individual minds and collective culture? And why do they wear the costume of 'aliens' in our technological age just as they once appeared as fairies, demons, or _devas_?

This is not dismissal. It is elevation.

Treating UAPs and abductions as psychological phenomena does not reduce them to 'hallucinations' or pathology. It invites serious, interdisciplinary study in psychology, anthropology, religious studies, and consciousness research, fields equipped to examine how the mind interfaces with realities richer than our everyday categories allow.

Two thinkers have mapped this terrain with uncommon rigour and respect for the data: Jacques Vallée and Jeffrey Kripal, particularly working in dialogue with experiencers like Whitley Strieber. Their framework offers a compelling way out of the physical-fixation trap.

Vallée, the French-born computer scientist, astronomer, and long-time UFO researcher, laid the groundwork decades ago. In _Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers_ (1969), he catalogued striking parallels between modern UFO encounters and historical accounts of fairy abductions, demonic visitations, and other folkloric intrusions.

The same motifs recur across centuries and cultures: beings from another realm who abduct humans, subject them to strange medical or sexual procedures, deliver cryptic messages, distort time, and then vanish, often leaving the witness transformed, terrified, or enlightened.

The phenomenon does not behave like visiting extraterrestrial hardware conducting scientific surveys. It behaves like a theatrical performance tailored to the cultural expectations of the era.

Vallée later refined this into his 'control system' hypothesis. The UAP phenomenon functions like a cybernetic feedback loop, an informational or behavioural system that introduces absurd, high-strangeness stimuli into human culture. These stimuli provoke awe, fear, belief shifts, and social responses. The system then 'reads' those responses and adjusts its next manifestations accordingly.

It is not a conspiracy of governments or ETs in the literal sense. It is a self-regulating mechanism, perhaps interdimensional but more probably archetypal, and emergent from consciousness itself, that subtly steers collective awareness.

The absurdity is not a flaw; it is a feature. By presenting narratives that defy easy categorisation (nuts-and-bolts craft one moment, telepathic entities the next), the phenomenon destabilises rigid worldviews and opens the psyche to new possibilities.

This explains why the latest War Department videos, for all their hype, add nothing new. They feed the mythic apparatus without resolving it. The blurry orbs and football shapes become modern fairy lights, symbols that interact with human expectation rather than independent physical objects demanding reverse engineering.

Enter Jeffrey Kripal, the Rice University historian of religion who has taken Vallée's archival impulse even further.

Kripal's collaboration with Whitley Strieber in _The Super Natural: A New Vision of the Unexplained_ (2016) reframes the entire discussion. Strieber, whose 1987 bestseller _Communion_ popularised the alien-abduction narrative, is not treated as a crank or a victim of delusion or, worse, a hoaxer.

His experiences (vivid encounters with nonhuman entities, implants, time distortion, and lifelong patterns of high strangeness) are taken seriously as real events. But they are real in a way that challenges both literalist ET believers and reductive materialists.

Kripal argues that what we call the 'supernatural' is actually a dimension of reality that is perfectly natural once we expand our categories beyond the mechanistic worldview of current naïve reality. The human mind is not a passive by-product of the brain; it acts as a filter or lens that ordinarily limits perception to the everyday world.

Under certain conditions (trauma, illness, near-death states, or spontaneous altered consciousness), that filter can turn thin or even crack. What pours through is not fantasy but a richer, fiercely alive reality populated by intelligences or energies that our culture currently lacks language to describe without distortion.

Strieber's abductions become case studies in this process. The entities that appeared as grey aliens in the 1980s were not necessarily extraterrestrials from another planet; they were visitors from a liminal domain that adopts the cultural costume most likely to get our attention.

In earlier eras they appeared as fairies or demons. Today they appear as extraterrestrials because that is the mythic vocabulary of a technological age. The encounter is traumatic precisely because the ego resists dissolution; when the ego surrenders, the same phenomenon can feel ecstatic or redemptive.

Kripal has been trying to institutionalise this approach through Rice University's 'Archives of the Impossible', which houses Vallée's papers alongside Strieber's correspondence and thousands of experiencer accounts. The archive treats these materials not as evidence of crashed saucers but as raw phenomenological data on the human encounter with the impossible.

Abduction experiences illustrate the psychological depth most vividly. The narrative template (being taken aboard a craft, subjected to invasive procedures by small grey beings with large black eyes, receiving messages about humanity's future, and returning with missing time) maps eerily onto well-documented neurological and psychological phenomena.

Sleep paralysis, accompanied by hypnopompic hallucinations of shadowy presences, chest pressure, and sensed intelligences, produces near-identical accounts. Hypnotic regression, long used to 'recover' abduction memories, is notoriously prone to false-memory formation, especially when the hypnotist holds strong prior beliefs. Cultural priming turns personal anomalies into coherent alien dramas.

Yet calling these experiences 'psychological' does not make them unreal or insignificant. On the contrary. They reveal the psyche's extraordinary plasticity and its capacity to interface with dimensions of reality that transcend ordinary consensus.

Trauma survivors frequently report heightened sensitivity to anomalous phenomena; the same mechanisms that produce dissociation or hypervigilance may also open perceptual doors. The abduction drama, for some, becomes a symbolic arena in which the psyche processes existential terror, ecological anxiety, or spiritual longing.

For others, it functions as genuine initiation, shattering the ego and forcing a confrontation with the 'super natural'.

The control-system lens explains the broader cultural function. Each wave of sightings or abductions coincides with periods of societal stress or paradigm shift.

The 1947 'flying saucer' wave emerged amid the dawn of the nuclear age and the Cold War. The 1960s–'70s contactee era mirrored countercultural longing for cosmic brotherhood. The 1980s–'90s abduction surge paralleled anxieties over biotechnology, reproductive technologies, and environmental collapse.

The phenomenon does not merely reflect culture; it actively shapes it, introducing destabilising symbols that provoke reflection, fear, or transformation. Governments' so-called disclosures of grainy videos, bundled with promises of more to come, become part of the feedback loop, sustaining belief without resolution.

This psychological turn dismantles conspiracy theories without insulting experiencers. There is no need to posit secret crash-retrieval programmes or interstellar tourists when the data fit a model of consciousness interacting with a richer informational or archetypal field.

It also sidesteps the dead-end literalism that demands physical wreckage while ignoring the transformative power of the encounters themselves. People are not lying about what they experienced. Their brains are doing what human brains have always done under the right conditions: generating coherent, emotionally charged narratives that interface with something genuinely 'other'.

No thinker articulated this compensatory mechanism more powerfully than Carl Jung. In his 1958 book _Flying Saucers: A Modern Myth of Things Seen in the Skies_, the Swiss psychologist examined the UFO phenomenon not as hardware from the stars but as a modern myth erupting from the collective unconscious.

Jung zeroed in on the circular, disc-shaped 'flying saucers' that dominated early reports. He saw in them a direct analogue to the _mandala_, an ancient, cross-cultural symbol of psychic totality and wholeness.

The _mandala_, Jung wrote, represents the Self: the integrated centre of the personality that transcends the ego and unites conscious and unconscious opposites. It is the archetype at the heart of individuation, the lifelong process by which a person becomes a complete, balanced individual.

In an era of profound psychic dissociation (Jung was writing amid nuclear terror, ideological polarisation, and the collapse of traditional religious certainties), the collective unconscious compensates by projecting symbols of order and salvation into the sky. UFOs, whether physically real or not, function as "impressive manifestations of totality".

Their rounded, luminous forms are _mandalas_ in motion: visions of wholeness that the fragmented modern psyche desperately needs but cannot yet locate within itself. Jung did not rule out a possible material basis for some sightings, but he insisted the psychological and mythic dimension was far more significant and empirically accessible.

The saucer becomes, in his words, "a spaceship controlled by an intelligent being", the rounded wholeness of the _mandala_ now dressed in technological garb.

This framework resonates powerfully today.

Jung's Cold War anxieties have been replaced by our own: artificial intelligence reshaping human identity, climate collapse threatening civilisation, geopolitical fractures, the rise of mavericks like Trump, algorithmic echo chambers that isolate rather than integrate, and a secular-fundamentalist cultural dialectics which is starved of transcendent meaning.

In 2026, the 8 May 'War Department' release (the same grainy "football-shaped bodies" and glowing orbs) serves as the latest _mandala_ projection. The hype surrounding it may be media sensationalism and the diversionary tactics of a failing political administration; yet the deep need to believe is the psyche's attempt to manufacture cosmic order when earthly integration feels impossible.

It is the collective unconscious at work, constellating symbols of wholeness because we remain split.

Even a committed sceptic like Carl Sagan found Jung's psychological lens compelling in its explanatory power. Sagan, who coined the dictum "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence", repeatedly acknowledged that the persistence of UFO beliefs and contact tales fulfilled deep human psychological needs, for superior beings, salvation, or cosmic meaning.

In the 1968 UFO Symposium and his writings, he noted Jung's insight that such phenomena speak to our longing for guidance amid uncertainty. Sagan never endorsed literal extraterrestrials or archetypal projections as proof, but he respected the framework as a serious account of why the myth endures when physical evidence does not.

The blurry dots satisfy a hunger that data alone cannot touch. Abduction experiences fit seamlessly into the Jungian picture. What appear as literal medical ordeals by grey aliens are, at a deeper level, symbolic dramas of ego dissolution and reintegration, the very stuff of individuation.

Fixating on hardware and government secrets has produced decades of disappointment and eroded public trust. A psychological and phenomenological approach opens productive avenues: rigorous study of altered states, comparative religion, trauma psychology, and the neurophenomenology of belief.

It honours the ethical imperative to support experiencers, not by validating literal alien probes but by validating the profundity of their inner journeys. It invites scientists, artists, and philosophers to collaborate on what Kripal calls "thinking impossibly", expanding our models of mind and reality without abandoning empirical rigour.

The universe remains vast and mysterious. If non-human intelligence exists elsewhere, unambiguous evidence will eventually arrive through replicable, high-resolution data. Until then, we do truth a disservice by pretending every fuzzy infrared orb is a visiting spacecraft.

Such a need to believe without understanding the deeper processes which produce that need can allow governments to manipulate citizens. The 8 May release and the marketing around it are the same old exercises in misdirection.

The real disclosure has always been internal. It lives in the minds of those who have stared into the impossible and returned changed. It lives in the cultural myths we weave around those encounters. And it lives in the quiet recognition that the boundary between 'natural' and 'super natural' is more semi-permeable than we have been taught to believe.
