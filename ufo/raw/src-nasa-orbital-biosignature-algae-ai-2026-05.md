# Orbital Biosignature Recon: NASA-developed AI Could Help Track Harmful Algae

NASA scientists have developed an artificial intelligence tool to address a persistent challenge in ocean waters. Research published in AGU Earth and Space Science demonstrated that the tool successfully fused data from multiple satellites to detect harmful algal blooms occurring in western Florida and Southern California.

Severe blooms create significant health risks and cost U.S. coastal economies tens of millions of dollars annually. Florida regions including Tampa Bay and Sarasota have struggled with this issue for decades. Karenia brevis thrives in Gulf waters, producing harmful algal blooms that kill wildlife, contaminate beaches, and sicken swimmers. On the West Coast, Pseudo-nitzschia blooms have poisoned hundreds of dolphins, California sea lions, and other marine mammals in recent years. Algal toxins can become airborne and cause respiratory illness in humans.

Health agencies regularly test waters and issue warnings or beach closures when needed. NOAA collaborates with states and local partners to issue harmful algal bloom forecasts during bloom seasons.

Collecting water samples manually requires hours on boats, with samples sent to labs for analysis—a process taking a day or more and requiring multiple tests. Determining where to test before a bloom spreads proves even more challenging.

NASA's Earth-orbiting satellites already track harmful algal blooms globally. The new AI tool, by consolidating diverse datasets, could serve as a force multiplier helping communities focus their efforts.

The research team—Michelle Gierach from NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, Kelly Luis from NASA JPL, and data scientist Nick LaHaye from Spatial Informatics Group—integrated findings from five space missions or instruments, including PACE and TROPOMI.

The primary challenge involved handling massive quantities of raw data. The team developed a self-supervised machine learning system designed to learn patterns from multiple satellite data types and compare them with field observations. This approach enables AI to recognize relationships between different data sources without requiring advance labeling.

The system was trained on satellite data from 2018 and 2019. Field and lab measurements added real-world context to recognized patterns. Scientists evaluated the tool across later time periods in the same geographic areas. Initial results demonstrate it can correctly identify and map harmful blooms, including specific species like K. brevis, performing effectively in complex coastal waters with sediment, plants, and runoff.

The team is now improving the tool using data from additional coastlines and expanding tests to other water bodies, including lakes, aiming to make it accessible to decision-makers in coming years.

Notable quotes:

Michelle Gierach: "At the very least, a tool like this can help us know where and when to collect water samples as an algal bloom is starting."

Nadya Vinogradova Shiffer: "Applying self-supervised AI to massive streams of satellite data is rapidly becoming a powerful tool for generating actionable ocean intelligence."

Kelly Luis: "The aim of this work is to start to bridge technologies to better serve end users and their needs, from aquaculture to tourism."
