# Pentagon's $500 Million Counter-Drone Deal Turns Ukraine's Cheap-Interceptor Math Into U.S. Doctrine

The Pentagon awarded Perennial Autonomy a three-year contract valued up to $500 million for drone-killing interceptors, marking the largest single counter-unmanned-aircraft deal the U.S. military has signed. Joint Interagency Task Force 401 announced the award on May 18, covering three platforms: the Merops interceptor, the Bumblebee quadcopter, and the Hornet midrange strike drone.

All three systems were combat-tested before the contract existed. Former Google CEO Eric Schmidt's company built the Merops to counter Russian Shahed attack drones over Ukraine, and the same hardware now protects U.S. forces in the Middle East against Iranian-made versions. The article previously detailed the Merops and its reported kill rates on January 6.

The contract documents a significant doctrine shift. U.S. forces initially fired million-dollar missiles at $30,000 drones during the opening week of the Iran war, then switched to a $15,000 interceptor instead. This contract locks that choice into standing American procurement.

JIATF-401 structured the deal as an indefinite-delivery, indefinite-quantity contract with a $500 million ceiling, allowing the Pentagon to place orders as needs arise over three years rather than committing to a fixed quantity upfront. This arrangement continues until the money is spent or three years pass, whichever occurs first. By Defense Daily's count, it represents the largest counter-drone contract the department has issued.

Brigadier General Matt Ross, director of JIATF-401, stated that the partnership gives the joint force "state-of-the-art, counter-UAS capability to remain lethal on today's modern battlefield." The task force noted that all three platforms are "currently being employed by forces operating in U.S. Central Command."

Perennial Autonomy, the current name of Schmidt's defense startup launched quietly in 2023, developed the Merops interceptor specifically for Ukrainian crews fighting Russian Shahed drones years before any U.S. service branch placed an order. The company maintained a low profile while remaining active in both Ukraine and CENTCOM.

The chronology is significant because it explains why the hardware was ready when Washington needed it. Schmidt initially established the venture as White Stork in 2023, following meetings with Ukrainian officials in 2022. It became Project Eagle in February 2024, then rebranded to Perennial Autonomy this year. The company recruited former Pentagon innovation chief Will Roper and engineers from Apple and SpaceX, among other firms. Perennial reports that the Merops has intercepted more than 4,000 Russian drones in Ukraine since mid-2024, describing the equipment as "battlefield-tested and proven at scale in Ukraine."

The interceptor, also called the Surveyor, features a fixed-wing design flying at more than 280 km/h (175 mph) according to Defense Express—fast enough to intercept jet-powered Shahed variants Russia currently fields. A pilot guides it toward the target, then the interceptor completes the engagement independently using onboard computer vision and radio-frequency detection while jam-resistant links maintain control during electronic warfare.

The Shahed-type drone that attacks Ukrainian cities also forms Iran's arsenal core. When the U.S.-Israeli operation against Iran began, CENTCOM forces confronted the same cost-imbalance equation Ukraine had been solving for two years: inexpensive attacking drones versus expensive defenses. This cost disparity explains why a Silicon Valley interceptor designed for Kyiv ended up protecting American assets in the Gulf.

Army Secretary Dan Driscoll told lawmakers in April that the service purchased 13,000 Merops interceptors in the conflict's first days at roughly $15,000 per unit, a price he said helped address the imbalance. According to his figures, a Shahed costs $30,000 to $50,000 to build, making a Merops that destroys one cheaper than the threat itself.

However, cheaper than a missile differs from truly cheap. Ukrainian-built interceptors such as Wild Hornets' Sting cost closer to $2,000 to $2,500, a figure documented in previous coverage. The Merops carries autonomy and AI layers those budget interceptors lack, and that capability justifies its premium. The premium is substantial—roughly six to seven times the Ukrainian price.

Perennial established European manufacturing through a partnership with Munich-based Twentyfour Industries to produce the Merops in Germany, adding capacity as European buyers queue up. Lithuania purchased 48 Merops in April, and Polish and Romanian forces have already trained and deployed with the system on NATO's eastern flank.

The contract sits within a broader Pentagon shift toward cheap, attritable drones. The Drone Dominance program—approximately a $1 billion Pentagon initiative launched in December 2025 to procure hundreds of thousands of small attack drones—operates on identical logic, treating drones as consumable supplies rather than durable aircraft. That program launched in December 2025.

JIATF-401 had previously awarded Perennial a separate $5.2 million contract in January for the Bumblebee V2, and the Army tested the Hornet in March. A Perennial spokesperson declined to specify how the systems performed in the Middle East or how many units the $500 million is expected to produce.

## DroneXL's Take

The headline number totals $500 million. The more revealing figure—which Perennial refuses to disclose—is how many interceptors that actually purchases. The procurement pattern assembled over six months shows the Pentagon's DOGE unit seizing drone buying last October, the Drone Dominance program launching in December, and the Army making an emergency 13,000-unit Merops purchase when the Iran war started. This contract constitutes the consolidation step, transforming urgent one-off purchases into a standing, enterprise-wide line of credit—precisely what an open-ended IDIQ with a $500 million ceiling accomplishes.

The throughline has consistently centered on cost. Lieutenant Colonel Pavlo Verkhovod of Ukraine's 25th Airborne Brigade framed the doctrine plainly: "There is little point in shooting down 100% of the drones if the interceptor costs more than the target."

A critical unanswered question remains. Driscoll's $15,000 Merops beats a missile, but it remains several times the price of a Ukrainian Sting performing comparable work, with the gap representing the AI and autonomy cheaper drones cannot yet carry. Whether $15,000 represents the floor or merely entry pricing, and whether co-production with Twentyfour Industries in Germany reduces unit costs or simply increases volume, will become apparent through subsequent orders against this ceiling. Perennial's refusal to disclose unit counts means the answer must be read backward from spending figures, with focus on obligation data rather than press releases.
