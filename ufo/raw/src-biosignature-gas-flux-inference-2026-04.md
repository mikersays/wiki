# Toward Inferring the Surface Fluxes of Biosignature Gases on Rocky Exoplanets from Telescope Spectra

The James Webb Space Telescope and the future Habitable Worlds Observatory are designed to identify exoplanet atmospheric spectra indicative of life. Most current spectral retrieval algorithms attempt to measure biogenic gas abundances from these spectra.

However, abundances present interpretive challenges since photochemistry, climate, and atmospheric escape alter their concentrations. To address this limitation, researchers developed a method for determining planetary surface gas fluxes by inverting a coupled photochemical-climate model.

As a proof-of-concept demonstration, the team applied their approach to a synthetic 10-transit JWST NIRSpec Prism spectrum of TRAPPIST-1 e, assuming it harbors a biosphere resembling the Archean Earth's. The retrieval successfully identified CO2 and CH4 and could constrain the methane flux to within approximately 1.5 orders of magnitude (68% credible interval), contingent upon accurate knowledge of TRAPPIST-1's near-UV spectrum.

Inferred surface gas fluxes enable probabilistic life detection assessment. In their nominal test case, approximately 80% of the posterior distribution for surface gas flux aligns with CH4-producing metabolism. The researchers acknowledge that results depend on various forward model assumptions inherent to any inverse problem.

The authors contend that enhancing exoplanet life detection requires progressing beyond atmospheric abundance measurements toward determining the surface fluxes sustaining them.

## Key Findings

- "The retrieval confidently detects CO2 and CH4 and can constrain the flux of CH4 into the atmosphere" within approximately 1.5 orders of magnitude.
- "~80% of the surface gas flux posterior is consistent with a CH4-producing metabolism" in their test scenario.
- "Increasing the robustness of life detection on exoplanets requires moving beyond atmospheric abundances toward inference of the surface fluxes."
