# Pentagon's AARO Quietly Held an Invite-Only Workshop to Help Shape the Future of UAP Research
**Source:** https://defensescoop.com/2026/03/16/dod-ufo-workshop-uap-research-aaro/
**Author:** Brandi Vincent
**Date:** March 16, 2026

## Overview

In early August 2025, the Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) quietly sponsored an exclusive, invite-only workshop that brought together approximately 40 participants from government, academic, and independent research backgrounds. The event was hosted by Associated Universities, Inc. in the Washington, D.C. area and lasted two days. The Defense Department covered lodging and meal expenses for attendees, though researchers funded their own travel costs. The workshop's existence was not widely publicized at the time, and details emerged only months later through DefenseScoop's reporting in March 2026.

## Purpose and Structure

The workshop was designed to standardize processes for capturing, sharing, and studying narrative data related to unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP). "Narrative data" in this context refers to qualitative information including witness testimony, historical accounts, military logs, and civilian reports -- as distinct from quantitative sensor data like radar tracks or infrared imagery.

The two-day event included:

- **Networking opportunities** allowing government researchers to interact with academic and independent investigators
- **Formal presentations** on current data collection methodologies and their limitations
- **Panel discussions** addressing challenges in UAP data standardization
- **Breakout sessions** exploring best practices for collecting, storing, and integrating UAP reports from diverse sources including military logs, civilian testimony, and archival records

The invite-only nature of the workshop meant participant privacy was prioritized, and no comprehensive public attendee list was released. This approach reflects AARO's delicate position: the office must balance transparency demands from Congress and the public with the need to protect sources, methods, and classified information.

## Key Findings: Five Cross-Cutting Conclusions

AARO published a whitepaper summarizing the workshop's outcomes, which outlined five cross-cutting conclusions that are expected to guide future UAP research and data management efforts:

### 1. Standards Development

The workshop concluded that "effective progress requires clear standards and common reporting templates, with robust metadata" that captures essential details such as location, time, environmental conditions, object morphology, and observer credibility. Currently, UAP reports arrive in widely varying formats depending on whether they originate from military personnel, commercial pilots, law enforcement, or civilian observers. This inconsistency makes cross-referencing and pattern analysis extremely difficult. Standardized templates would enable AARO to build a more coherent and analyzable database.

### 2. Data Integration

Linking datasets across military and civilian sources requires balancing interoperability with privacy and classification constraints. Military sensor data is often classified at high levels, while civilian reports may contain personally identifiable information. The workshop explored technical and policy frameworks that would allow meaningful integration of these disparate data streams without compromising either national security or individual privacy. This challenge is particularly acute when military encounters correlate with civilian sightings of the same phenomenon, as cross-referencing could reveal classified sensor capabilities.

### 3. Credibility Assessment

Corroboration was identified as the primary credibility measure for UAP reports. A sighting reported by a single observer with no sensor data is treated differently from one confirmed by multiple witnesses and multiple sensor modalities. However, the workshop recognized that manual credibility assessment does not scale to AARO's growing caseload of over 2,000 cases. Automated filtering methods are needed to efficiently triage incoming reports, but these must be carefully designed to avoid prematurely dismissing potentially significant cases.

### 4. AI and Machine Learning Implementation

Machine learning tools offer significant capabilities for UAP research, including automated transcription of witness interviews, pattern recognition across large datasets, and anomaly detection in sensor data. However, the workshop emphasized that these tools "demand careful deployment to prevent bias and hallucinations." Participants stressed that human oversight must remain essential at every stage -- AI can assist in processing and flagging data, but human analysts must make final determinations about case significance and classification. This conclusion reflects broader concerns about AI reliability in high-stakes analytical contexts.

### 5. Community Engagement

Building a sustainable, interdisciplinary research community through continued collaboration was identified as essential for strengthening UAP investigation efforts. The workshop represented an unusual instance of AARO actively engaging with non-government researchers, and participants argued that this model should be expanded. Academic researchers bring methodological rigor and peer review standards, while independent investigators often possess deep subject matter expertise and access to historical case files not held by the government.

## Implications for Future Operations

AARO indicated that the workshop's findings "may influence how and where technical sensors are deployed." This statement suggests that the narrative data analysis could help identify geographic or temporal patterns that inform the placement of AARO's sensor suites -- purpose-built detection systems designed to capture high-quality data on UAP in the field. If narrative reports cluster in certain locations or follow temporal patterns, AARO could strategically position sensors to maximize the probability of capturing hard data on repeat phenomena.

## Future Direction

Pentagon spokesperson Sue Gough stated that the office hopes to convene future workshops to foster the interdisciplinary research community that participants envisioned. However, no additional events were announced at the time of publication (March 2026). The workshop represents a notable shift in AARO's posture -- from a primarily reactive investigative body to one that is proactively shaping research methodology and building external partnerships.

## Significance

This workshop is significant for several reasons:

- It represents AARO's first known effort to formally engage non-government researchers in shaping its data collection and analysis methodologies
- The published whitepaper provides the most detailed public insight into AARO's analytical challenges and priorities
- The emphasis on AI tools and standardized reporting suggests AARO is preparing to scale its operations significantly
- The acknowledgment that narrative data (witness testimony) has research value alongside sensor data represents a methodological evolution for the office
