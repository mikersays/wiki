**EIRP** (Equivalent / Effective Isotropic Radiated Power) is the standard metric used in radio [[seti|SETI]] and [[technosignatures|technosignature]] searches to express transmitter detectability. It is the power that a hypothetical *isotropic* (omnidirectional) transmitter would need to radiate to produce the signal a search could detect at a given distance. A survey's **EIRP upper limit** therefore states the weakest beacon that survey could have ruled out — lower limits mean a more sensitive search.

## Why it matters

- EIRP lets searches with different telescopes, frequencies, distances, and integration times be compared on a common footing, since raw flux limits alone don't capture how far away or how powerful a putative transmitter is.
- For reference scale: a planetary-radar transmitter like Arecibo had an EIRP of roughly 10¹³ W; a Kardashev Type I civilization commanding its planet's power budget could in principle exceed 10¹⁷ W.

## Vault appearances

- The [[sardinia-radio-telescope|Sardinia Radio Telescope]] high-frequency survey set an EIRP limit of **1.8×10¹⁹ W** over ~5×10⁵ stars toward the [[galactic-center|Galactic Center]] ([[src-sardinia-technosignature-search-2026-05]]).
- By contrast, the [[src-fast-3i-atlas-technosignature-search-2026-03|FAST search of the nearby interstellar object 3I/ATLAS]] constrained transmitter power to as low as ~2.9×10⁻³ W — vastly tighter because the target was astronomically close.
