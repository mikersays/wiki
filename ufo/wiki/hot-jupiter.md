A **hot Jupiter** is a gas-giant exoplanet orbiting very close to its host star — often closer than Mercury orbits the Sun — and therefore intensely heated and irradiated. Such planets are typically **tidally locked**, with a permanent dayside and nightside separated by extreme limb-to-limb temperature contrasts. Their hot, cloud- and haze-prone atmospheres have historically confounded [[transmission-spectroscopy]] composition retrievals.

## Significance for atmosphere studies

Hot Jupiters are the most accessible exoplanets for atmospheric characterization: large, hot, and frequently transiting bright stars. They serve as laboratories for cloud physics and 3D atmospheric dynamics — see [[exoplanet-atmosphere-models]].

The [[jwst]] limb-resolved study of [[wasp-94ab]] ([[src-jwst-wasp-94ab-weather-cycle-2026-05]]) showed that a **morning-cloud / evening-clear asymmetry** recurs across multiple hot Jupiters (WASP-94Ab, WASP-39b, WASP-17b). Because earlier instruments averaged both limbs together, this asymmetry biased inferred metallicity — WASP-94Ab's oxygen/carbon abundance was overstated by orders of magnitude until the limbs were separated. The lesson generalizes: limb asymmetry must be accounted for in any transit spectrum, including those of smaller [[biosignatures|biosignature]] targets.
