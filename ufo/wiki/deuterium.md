# Deuterium / Deuterium-to-Hydrogen (D/H) Ratio

**Deuterium** is a stable isotope of hydrogen with one proton and one neutron; water made with it ("heavy water," HDO or D₂O) is measurably heavier than ordinary H₂O. The **deuterium-to-hydrogen (D/H) ratio** of an icy body is a chemical fingerprint of the temperature and radiation environment in which it formed — colder, lower-radiation regions favor higher deuterium enrichment.

## Significance for interstellar objects

Per [[src-3i-atlas-heavy-water-2026-05]], the interstellar comet [[3i-atlas]] shows a D/H ratio roughly **30× higher than solar-system comets** and **~40× higher than Earth's oceans** — the highest of any known planetary system or comet. The measurement, made via MDM Observatory gas detections plus [[alma]] submillimeter spectroscopy, was the first deuterated-water analysis of an [[interstellar-objects|interstellar object]] and implies 3I/ATLAS formed in a far colder birthplace than the solar nebula.

The D/H ratio is also central to the long-standing question of whether Earth's water was delivered by comets: comparing cometary D/H against Earth's ocean value tests that hypothesis. An interstellar comet with a wildly different ratio underscores how formation environments vary between planetary systems.
