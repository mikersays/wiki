# Infobae: The True Origin of the Buga Sphere

## Summary

Infobae Colombia synthesis (Oct 13 2025) of the [[buga-sphere]] case: a metallic ball reportedly found March 2025 near [[buga]], Valle del Cauca, by [[jose-arias-restrepo|José Arias Restrepo]], passed to his cousin [[david-velez-el-potro|David Vélez "El Potro"]] in Cali, and handed to [[jaime-maussan]] in Mexico. The article's "verdadero origen" thesis is not a single explanation but the **convergent verdict of Colombian astronomers** that the sphere is a recent terrestrial artifact — most plausibly a publicity stunt or a man-made "drovni" (drone-UFO), not extraterrestrial. Four named scientists — [[santiago-vargas]], [[marino-guarin]], [[oscar-amaya-montoya]], and [[lauren-flor-torres]] — assess the materials as "very terrestrial" (notably **optical fibers**), reject the unattributed 12,560-year carbon-14 claim, and point to the **absence of atmospheric friction marks** that any genuine meteorite would carry. The article also surfaces a competing terrestrial provenance: an unnamed Bogotá laboratory told Vélez the sphere was part of an experiment begun in 2011.

## Key Claims

- The Buga Sphere was found March 2025 in the Alto Bonito sector of Buga by Arias using a metal detector; an 8-minute phone video shows it flying "like a motorized ball" before striking power lines and falling.
- Arias reported weight inflation (2 → 10 kg) and post-handling symptoms (dizziness, vomiting, metallic taste); cousin Vélez retrieved it in a lead suit and stored it 2 months at his Cali metal-detector business.
- Vélez's "Cazadores de Ovnis Latinoamérica" Facebook page seeded a viral narrative including ~170 alleged sphere-sighting videos across Medellín, Tuluá, Yumbo — including claims that "the spheres have caused airport closures."
- Maussan's team took the sphere to Mexico; Vélez claims a **carbon-14 result of 12,560 years** and polymer analysis, but no documentation has been produced.
- **[[santiago-vargas]]** (Observatorio Astronómico, Universidad Nacional): the sphere's compounds are "very terrestrial," citing identified **optical fibers**; dismisses any otherworldly technology.
- **[[marino-guarin]]** (Escuela de Astronomía de Cali): calls the case "cuentos" and "charlatanería," explicitly framing it as a publicity stunt ("montaje con fines publicitarios").
- **[[oscar-amaya-montoya]]** (Fundación Casa de la Ciencia de Buga): "probable that this is a recent technological artifact manufactured on Earth."
- **[[lauren-flor-torres]]** (PhD Astrophysics, Universidad de Antioquia): meteorites entering the atmosphere show friction alteration; the Buga sphere "luce impecable" — disqualifying a meteoritic origin.
- An unnamed Bogotá laboratory reportedly told Vélez the sphere was part of an experiment begun in 2011 (rejected by Vélez but presented as a credible terrestrial provenance).
- Maussan's prior claims (Peruvian [[nazca-mummies|"non-human" mummies]]) were debunked as composites of terrestrial materials — pattern relevant to Buga.
