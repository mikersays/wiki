# Radiocarbon Dating

**Radiocarbon (carbon-14) dating** measures C-14 decay in organic material to estimate the time since the organism stopped exchanging carbon with the atmosphere. **Accelerator Mass Spectrometry (AMS)** is the high-sensitivity modern variant, capable of dating sub-milligram samples up to ~50,000 years old.

## Scope and limits

- Only ages **organic** material (plant matter, bone, resin, charcoal, shell).
- Cannot directly date inorganic objects (metals, ceramics, manufactured composites) unless intrinsic organics are demonstrably **contemporaneous with the object's formation**.
- **Dating organic *contents* of an inorganic *container* does not date the container** — the contents may post-date, pre-date, or have been introduced after recovery.
- Contamination by modern carbon (handling, conservation resins, environmental exposure) can inflate apparent youth; contamination by older carbon (limestone, "old wood") can inflate apparent age.

## Validation requirements

A scientifically usable C-14 result requires:

- Published laboratory report.
- Named lab and named personnel / PI.
- Calibration data and stated error margins.
- Documented sample-prep methodology and a clean [[chain-of-custody]].
- Ideally: independent replication and peer-reviewed publication.

## Relevance to UAP / recovered-artifact claims

[[radiocarbon-dating|C-14 dating]] is used as a recurring rhetorical move in [[crash-retrieval-programs|recovered-artifact]] presentations to imply non-terrestrial antiquity. The [[buga-sphere]] case is a current example: a claimed **~12,560-year date** is attributed to the [[university-of-georgia]] for organic resin inside the sphere ([[src-buga-sphere-uga-carbon-date-claim-2025-10]]), but per [[src-buga-sphere-carbon-dating-debunk-2026-04]] no UGA documentation exists, and even if accurate the resin date does not date the inorganic sphere. Colombian astronomer [[marino-guarin]] notes that no "official report" has been published by any university for the sphere.
