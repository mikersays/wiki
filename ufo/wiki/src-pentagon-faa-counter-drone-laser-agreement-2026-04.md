## Summary

The Pentagon and [[faa]] signed a "landmark" safety agreement over the domestic use of [[counter-uas]] [[directed-energy-weapons|laser technology]], following two back-to-back airspace incidents in Texas in February 2026 and a March 7–8 safety test at [[white-sands-missile-range]]. Government evaluators tested the Army Multi-Purpose High Energy Laser (**AMP-HEL**) against a decommissioned Boeing 767 fuselage and demonstrated its automated safety shut-off system, which inhibited firing when commercial aircraft signals were detected. FAA Administrator Bryan Bedford confirmed the systems "do not present an increased risk to the flying public." [[jiatf-401]] director Brig. Gen. [[matt-ross]] said the agreement's framework will be used "from the start" for any counter-drone systems employed in the U.S. Reuters identified the laser used in the February Texas incidents as a LOCUST system built by [[aerovironment]].

## Key Claims

- Pentagon and FAA jointly determined counter-drone laser safety controls "do not pose undue risk to passenger aircraft" (joint release)
- March 7–8 White Sands test fired the AMP-HEL at maximum effective range against a decommissioned Boeing 767 fuselage and flew multiple aircraft behind simulated targets (DefenseScoop reporting)
- The AMP-HEL's automated safety system detected a civilian aircraft approaching an Albuquerque airport miles away from the range and automatically shut down (Col. [[scott-mclellan]])
- In the first February Texas incident, [[customs-and-border-protection|CBP]] personnel used a Pentagon-loaned laser near El Paso to shoot down an object later identified as Mylar balloons (DefenseScoop)
- In the second incident, soldiers shot down a CBP-operated drone near [[fort-hancock]] because CBP failed to notify the military task force (article reporting)
- Reuters identified the border laser as a LOCUST system built by [[aerovironment]] (Reuters)
- More than 1,000 drone incursions occur at the southern border per month (military officials)
