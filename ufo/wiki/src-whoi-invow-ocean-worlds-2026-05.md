## Summary

WHOI press release announcing that [[nasa]] has selected the **Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution** to lead the five-year **[[invow|Investigating Ocean Worlds (InvOW)]]** project (~$5M, starting 2026) to improve the analysis of carbon-rich molecules as potential biological indicators on [[ocean-worlds]] like [[europa-clipper|Europa]] and [[enceladus]]. PI [[chris-german]] (WHOI senior scientist) frames the work as preparing for [[europa-clipper]]'s 2030 arrival by characterizing how physical, chemical, and possibly biological processes modify [[biosignatures|organic-carbon signals]] as they travel from the rocky subseafloor through the ocean to the icy cryosphere. The 16-laboratory team spans ocean, polar, and space sciences and integrates theoretical modelling, lab experiments, and fieldwork. Deputy PI [[tori-hoehler]] (Director, [[center-for-life-detection]] at [[nasa-ames-research-center]]) and co-Is Susan Lang (WHOI) and Brandy Toner (University of Minnesota) emphasize the "needle-in-a-haystack" signal-to-noise problem: on distant ocean worlds, non-biological organics may dominate, inverting Earth's situation. InvOW builds on the prior NASA-funded [[exow|Exploring Ocean Worlds (ExOW)]] initiative.

## Key Claims

- NASA selected [[whoi]] to lead InvOW; five-year award ~$5M; project begins 2026.
- InvOW spans 16 U.S. laboratories integrating ocean, polar, and space sciences across three ocean-world domains: rocky subseafloor, ocean, and icy cryosphere.
- Central scientific problem: identifying organic-carbon biosignatures unambiguously given that non-biological organics likely dominate on distant ocean worlds where energy for biology is scarce — inverting Earth's high-consumption signal-to-noise regime.
- InvOW explicitly times itself to Europa Clipper's 2030 Europa arrival, building interpretive groundwork for any organics it detects.
- InvOW is the successor to ExOW (also led by German), shifting focus from physical/geological processes to organic-molecule interpretation.
- Tori Hoehler (Deputy PI) directs NASA Ames's Center for Life Detection and frames the project as integrating planetary scientists with Earth oceanographers.

## Notable Quotes

> "If you are alive today, this is the first generation where the question of whether there is life elsewhere in the universe could be answered in your lifetime… it is entirely plausible that there is life out there, within humanity's reach, and we just need to go and look." — Chris German

> "On ocean worlds, identifying valid indicators of life might be a needle-in-a-haystack kind of problem… We need to rule out all of the other ways that signals could be generated, by non-life processes, so that they don't otherwise confound mission scientists." — Chris German

> "Bringing together planetary scientists and Earth oceanographers to understand alien oceans the way we understand our own — as complex systems where geology, physics, chemistry, and possibly biology, work together in concert." — Tori Hoehler
