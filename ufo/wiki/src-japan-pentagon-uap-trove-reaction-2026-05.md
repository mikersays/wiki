## Summary

IBTimes UK reports (May 13, 2026) that the [[japan|Japanese]] government is reviewing footage from the [[pursue|PURSUE Release 01]] trove that depict UAP incidents near Japanese airspace and the East China Sea. Chief Cabinet Secretary [[minoru-kihara]] confirmed at a Tokyo press conference that he had personally viewed the 161 declassified files released May 8 by the [[department-of-war]] under [[donald-trump]]'s declassification directive, and said Tokyo is analyzing the material with Washington and other partners. Cited footage includes a 2023 infrared clip catalogued as [[dow-uap-pr47]] ([[us-indo-pacific-command]]) showing multiple contrasting objects holding formation, and a 2024 clip of a "football-shaped" object with three radial protrusions (also documented in [[src-space-com-pursue-video-trove-2026-05]] and [[src-twz-pursue-shrug-2026-05]]). The story situates Japan's interest against the 2024 Defense Ministry posture shift, the 2023 Chinese spy-balloon context, and the bipartisan ~80-member [[japan-uap-parliamentary-group]] now pushing for a dedicated UAP office ([[japan-uap-framework]]). Kihara also signalled that any release of Japanese-held footage will be evaluated case-by-case to protect intelligence sources.

## Key Claims

- Chief Cabinet Secretary [[minoru-kihara]] has personally viewed the May 8 [[pursue|PURSUE Release 01]] files and confirmed Japan is reviewing them alongside the U.S. and other partners.
- The 161-file PURSUE release was triggered by [[donald-trump|Trump]]'s order for further declassification of Pentagon UAP records.
- Among the declassified materials are videos recorded during Indo-Pacific operations near Japanese airspace and the East China Sea, including a 2023 infrared clip ([[dow-uap-pr47]]) showing several contrasting objects in formation and a 2024 clip of a "football-shaped" object with protrusions.
- Kihara declined to detail US-Japan exchanges and said any future Japanese disclosure will be evaluated case-by-case against intelligence-protection concerns.
- Japan's Defence Ministry strongly suspects unidentified objects spotted over Japan from 2019-2021 were Chinese spy balloons, paralleling the 2023 U.S. shootdown.
- A bipartisan group of roughly 80 Japanese lawmakers ([[japan-uap-parliamentary-group]]) formed in 2024 to examine UAP-related security issues and has since called for a dedicated UAP government office focused on airspace monitoring.
- Japanese officials continue to frame UAP primarily as a national defence and airspace-surveillance issue rather than a non-human-intelligence question.
