# 3I/ATLAS Contains Anomalously Heavy (Deuterium-Rich) Water

## Summary

A University of Michigan–led study published in *Nature Astronomy* reports that the interstellar comet [[3i-atlas]] contains water with an extraordinarily high **[[deuterium]]** ratio — about 30× higher than comets native to our solar system and roughly 40× higher than Earth's oceans. Because the deuterium-to-hydrogen (D/H) ratio acts as a chemical fingerprint of formation conditions, the team concludes 3I/ATLAS formed in a region far colder and with lower radiation than the environment that produced our Sun's planets and comets. The measurement combined early gas-emission detections at the MDM Observatory (Arizona) with [[alma]] submillimeter observations in Chile, marking the first time deuterated-water analysis has been performed on an [[interstellar-objects|interstellar object]]. Lead author Luis Salazar Manzano and co-leader Teresa Paneque-Carreño frame the result as direct evidence that the conditions forming our solar system are not universal.

## Key Claims

- 3I/ATLAS water has a deuterium ratio ~30× higher than solar-system comets and ~40× higher than Earth's oceans — higher than seen in any other planetary system or comet (Salazar Manzano).
- The high D/H ratio implies formation in a much colder, lower-radiation region than the solar nebula (study conclusion).
- First-ever deuterated-water measurement on an interstellar object, enabled by MDM Observatory gas-emission detections plus [[alma]]'s ability to distinguish heavy from ordinary water.
- Early detection of 3I/ATLAS was essential to securing the detailed follow-up observations.
- Funded by NASA, the U.S. NSF, Chile's National Research and Development Agency, the Michigan Society of Fellows, and the Heising-Simons Foundation; published in *Nature Astronomy*.

## Notable Quotes

> "The amount of deuterium with respect to ordinary hydrogen in water is higher than anything we've seen before in other planetary systems and planetary comets." — Luis Salazar Manzano

> "This is proof that whatever the conditions were that led to the creation of our solar system are not ubiquitous throughout space. That may sound obvious, but it's one of those things that you need to prove." — Teresa Paneque-Carreño

> "We need to be taking care of our night skies and keeping them clear and dark so we can detect these tiny and faint objects." — Teresa Paneque-Carreño
