# Breaking the Taboo: Reviving Academic UAP, Abduction, and Experiencer Research

Program and speaker page for a free, online-only academic symposium on **Friday, July 10, 2026**, titled *"Breaking the Taboo: Reviving academic UAP, abduction, and experiencer research."* The event is co-organized by **[[karin-austin|Karin Austin]]**, **[[michael-bohlander|Michael Bohlander]]** (Durham University), and **[[kimberly-engels|Kimberly S. Engels]]** (Molloy University), and co-sponsored by the **[[center-for-the-impossible|Center For The Impossible]]** at Rice University and the **[[john-mack-institute|John Mack Institute]]**. The source page was captured in lieu of the originating Durham University Law School announcement ("Professor Bohlander co-organises symposium on contact with Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAP)"), which was CloudFront-blocked and unarchived.

The symposium positions itself as an effort to **re-legitimize academic study of the experiencer / "abduction" / contact phenomenon** — a research lineage descending from Harvard psychiatrist [[john-mack|John E. Mack]], whose archives now reside at Harvard and Rice. It complements [[src-academic-uap-stigma-2026-04|the broader academic-stigma critique]] but focuses specifically on the most heavily stigmatized subfield: first-person contact and abduction testimony.

## Summary

The program runs three thematic sessions. **Session 1 (The Experience and its Integration)** centers on experiencer testimony and mental-health support, with talks by Debra Jordan Kauble (Budd Hopkins's "Kathie Davis"), former Naval Intelligence analyst [[matthew-roberts|Matthew Roberts]], peer-support advocates Andrea Oddo and Charles Holt (co-founder of r/Experiencers), and JMI Clinical Director Simon Bresler on post-encounter "ontological shock." **Session 2 (Translating Experience into Knowledge)** covers Austin's metadata analysis of experiencer archives, Beth Glick's new **EXIM** (Exceptional Experience Integration & Impact Measure) instrument, and Tim Murithi's "Pan-African Ufology." **Session 3 (Normative Questions: The Ethics of Contact)** is the legal-philosophical core: Bohlander argues alien use of force in encounters may rise to **"core crimes under international criminal law,"** and Engels argues anthropocentric ethics are insufficient where one party holds knowledge unavailable to the other. [[jeffrey-kripal|Jeffrey Kripal]] (Rice) gives the welcome; Brenda Denzler responds.

## Key Claims

- The symposium frames experiencer/abduction research as a marginalized-but-revivable academic field, descending from [[john-mack|John Mack]]'s work; the John Mack archives were transferred to Harvard University and Rice's [[center-for-the-impossible|Center For The Impossible]] in 2022 (attributed to Karin Austin's bio).
- [[michael-bohlander]] argues that an alien intelligence overriding human autonomy in an encounter constitutes violence that "can rise in severity to the level of what these days are called core crimes under international criminal law."
- Karin Austin presents a "first-of-its-kind" metadata analysis of experiencer reports across multiple archival collections, drawing on "thousands of convergent accounts" and consistent worldview transformations.
- Beth Glick introduces **EXIM**, a multidimensional instrument measuring post-experience integration across identity, worldview, relationships, and behavior — noting both prosocial outcomes (interconnectedness, ecological concern) and negative ones (ego inflation, grandiosity, belief rigidification).
- [[matthew-roberts]], a former Office of Naval Intelligence cryptologist, states he was aboard the USS *Theodore Roosevelt* during collection of the Gimbal and Go Fast UAP footage and began having "follow-on experiences" afterward.
- Tim Murithi (ICER South African representative; Society for UAP Studies advisory board) presents "Pan-African Ufology," invoking the Dogon/Sirius B connection, Cynthia Hind's 1988 UFO Afrinews, and the Ariel School (Zimbabwe) case.

## Notable Quotes

> "A standard part of the narratives about abductions or other encounters of humans with alien intelligence is the use of force in the wider sense in overcoming human resistance. This paper argues that disregarding human autonomy makes these interactions into an exercise of violence on the part of the alien intelligence which can rise in severity to the level of what these days are called core crimes under international criminal law." — [[michael-bohlander|Michael Bohlander]], abstract

> "When the terms of the interaction itself exceed human conceptual grasp, conventional appeals to consent, autonomy, and harm require re-examination." — [[kimberly-engels|Kimberly S. Engels]], abstract
