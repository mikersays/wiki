## Summary

Malaysia's government tasked marine robotics firm [[ocean-infinity]] to resume the search for [[malaysia-airlines-flight-mh370]], a Boeing 777 that vanished in March 2014 with 239 people on board. The search targeted approximately 10,000 square miles of the southern Indian Ocean. [[ocean-infinity]] operated on a "no find, no fee" basis, receiving $70 million only if wreckage is located. University of Western Australia oceanographer Charitha Pattiaratchi noted that ocean-floor conditions favor preservation and that search technology has improved significantly since 2014.

## Key Claims

- Malaysia commissioned [[ocean-infinity]] to resume the MH370 search in the southern Indian Ocean (Malaysian government via NPR)
- The search area covers roughly 10,000 square miles, comparable in size to Tasmania or Sri Lanka (Prof. Charitha Pattiaratchi, University of Western Australia)
- Plane parts could still be discovered because ocean-floor conditions remain relatively stable (Pattiaratchi)
- Search technology has improved significantly since MH370's 2014 disappearance (Pattiaratchi)
- [[ocean-infinity]] would receive $70 million only if it locates the wreckage
