# Astrophyzix: Carbon-Dating Debunk of the Buga Sphere

## Summary

Astrophyzix article (Apr 25 2026) by Orion A. Vega methodologically rebutting the viral 2026 claim that the [[buga-sphere|Buga Sphere]] — co-presented by [[jaime-maussan]] and [[steven-greer]] at the May 8 [[the-disclosure-project]] NPC event ([[src-greer-disclosure-event-npc-2026-05]]) — has been Accelerator Mass Spectrometry (AMS) dated to ~12,560 years by the [[university-of-georgia]]. The piece argues that (a) [[radiocarbon-dating]] only ages organic samples, not the inorganic object that contained them; (b) no [[chain-of-custody]] or laboratory documentation has been produced; and (c) the reported "fibre-optic" / "advanced engineering" descriptions are interpretive, not backed by SEM/EDS/XRF analysis. Concludes the object should be treated as "unverified in origin and undated in any scientifically meaningful sense."

## Key Claims

- Radiocarbon (C-14) dating is limited to organic materials and was reportedly performed only on a resin-like substance found inside small cavities of the sphere — not the object itself.
- Even if the resin date is accurate, conflating resin age with object age is "a fundamental methodological error" — the resin may be non-original or post-discovery contamination.
- The claim that the University of Georgia conducted/confirmed the AMS analysis is unsupported: no published lab report, named researchers, calibration data, press release, or peer-reviewed publication.
- Descriptions of "fibre-optic-like structures" or "precision engineering" are not backed by Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS), X-ray Fluorescence (XRF), or mass spectrometry data.
- No documented [[chain-of-custody]], discovery context, or stratigraphic information has been published.
- Credibility requires: direct dating of primary material, full compositional/structural analysis, transparent lab methods, independent replication, and peer-reviewed publication — none of which have been met.

## Notable Quotes

> "Even if the radiocarbon result were accurate, it would only establish the age of the organic sample, not the object in which it was found. Conflating these two is a fundamental methodological error." — Orion A. Vega

> "There is no record of a press release, academic publication, or dataset associated with this claim. Without such documentation, the assertion of institutional confirmation cannot be substantiated." — Orion A. Vega

> "The object should therefore be regarded as unverified in origin and undated in any scientifically meaningful sense." — Orion A. Vega
