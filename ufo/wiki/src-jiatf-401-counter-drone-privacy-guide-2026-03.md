## Summary

The Army-led [[jiatf-401]] published a public guide titled "Counter-UAS Operations: Safeguarding Freedoms and Preserving Privacy," explaining how [[counter-uas]] detection technologies (radar, EO/IR, acoustic, RF) operate and comply with U.S. surveillance and communications law. The guide stresses that RF-based detection systems use passive "signal fingerprinting" — analyzing waveform characteristics rather than intercepting communication content — and are engineered with "privacy by design" principles including data minimization and automatic face/license-plate blurring. It cites the Electronic Communications Privacy Act, the Pen/Trap statute, and FCC rules as governing constraints. The publication aligns with the [[jiatf-401-marketplace]]'s initial operational capability and the Pentagon's Replicator 2 program.

## Key Claims

- Counter-UAS RF sensors are passive during normal operation — they "listen to signals already being transmitted" and do not emit disruptive signals or intercept communication content (JIATF-401 guide)
- Signal fingerprinting matches unknown emitters against a library of known drone signatures by analyzing frequency, signal strength, bandwidth, power levels, modulation, and timing — without decoding message content (JIATF-401 guide)
- Operational systems are "explicitly engineered to filter, truncate or discard communication content on reception" (JIATF-401 guide)
- Privacy features include real-time signal processing with no long-term content storage, and automatic blurring/anonymization of faces and license plates in imagery (Col. Scott Humr)
- The [[jiatf-401-marketplace]] has declared initial operational capability as a procurement platform for vetted C-UAS technologies (JIATF-401)

## Notable Quotes

> "Countering drones is not just a battlefield problem — it's a homeland defense imperative." — Brig. Gen. [[matt-ross]]

> "Our approach is to safeguard privacy by design. The technologies we are fielding are engineered to be effective while adhering to strict privacy principles." — Col. Scott Humr

> "The analysis of [radio frequency] signals to identify potential threats is legally permissible in the United States, provided that the methods used do not intercept the contents of private communications." — JIATF-401 guide
