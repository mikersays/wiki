## Summary

[[astrobiology-com]] ([[keith-cowing]]) summarizes a preprint (arXiv:2605.15337) by [[peter-m-higgins]] et al. presenting a quantitative framework for evaluating [[biosignatures]] on [[ocean-worlds]] against abiotic baselines. Using [[enceladus]] as the primary case study, the authors assess two candidate biosignature classes — [[carbon-isotope-biosignatures|carbon isotope ratios]] (delta-13C in CH4/CO2) and [[amino-acids|amino acid]] chirality — and find that current uncertainties in abiotic processes prevent either from definitively confirming a biosphere. The framework warns that neglecting the [[abiotic-baseline]] risks **false negative** life-detection claims (not just [[false-positive-biosignatures|false positives]]) for both isotopic and chiral biosignatures. Complementary geophysical observations — constraining internal temperatures to ~10-100 C, rheology, lithology, initial organic inventory, and ocean transport timescales — are required before interpreting biosignatures on Enceladus, [[europa-clipper|Europa]], [[titan]], and similar bodies.

## Key Claims

- Uncertainties in abiotic carbon-isotope cycling on Enceladus currently prevent hypothetical delta-13C measurements from definitively inferring a biosphere (Higgins et al.)
- Neglecting the [[abiotic-baseline]] risks **false negative** life-detection claims — a novel emphasis alongside the more commonly discussed false-positive problem (Higgins et al.)
- Amino acid chirality as a biosignature is also undermined by abiotic baseline uncertainties on ocean worlds (Higgins et al.)
- Interpreting biosignatures on Enceladus, Europa, Titan, and similar bodies requires complementary geophysical constraints: internal temperatures (~10-100 C), rheology, lithology, abiotic organic inventory, and ocean transport timescales (Higgins et al.)
- The paper develops a modular process-based model of carbon isotope cycling on Enceladus, tracking isotope enrichment factors across influx/outflux modules (Higgins et al.)
