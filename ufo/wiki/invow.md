# Investigating Ocean Worlds (InvOW)

Five-year (2026–2031) [[nasa]]-funded **~$5M** research project led by the [[whoi|Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution]]; 16-laboratory U.S. consortium integrating ocean, polar, and space sciences. Successor to the prior NASA-funded **[[exow|Exploring Ocean Worlds (ExOW)]]** initiative.

## Three-domain framework

InvOW splits the [[ocean-worlds|ocean-world]] organic-carbon problem across three sequential domains and tracks how [[biosignatures]] are modified at each interface:

1. **Rocky subseafloor** — production of organic molecules in seafloor hydrothermal systems.
2. **Ocean** — physical, chemical, and biological transformation in transit.
3. **Icy cryosphere** — final preservation/obscuration before detection by missions like [[europa-clipper]].

## Central problem

On distant ocean worlds where energy for biology is scarce, **non-biological organics may dominate**, inverting Earth's high-consumption signal-to-noise regime. This is a [[false-positive-biosignatures|false-positive]] class explicitly targeted by InvOW.

## Leadership

- **PI**: [[chris-german]] (WHOI senior scientist)
- **Deputy PI**: [[tori-hoehler]] (Director, [[center-for-life-detection]], [[nasa-ames-research-center]])

## Mission timing

InvOW explicitly times itself to **[[europa-clipper]]'s 2030 Europa arrival**, building interpretive groundwork for any organics it detects.
