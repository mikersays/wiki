# Hegseth Doubles Down on Trump's UAP Disclosure Promise as AARO's Caseload Exceeds 2,000
**Source:** https://defensescoop.com/2026/02/25/hegseth-ufo-disclosure-trump-aaro-uap-caseload/
**Author:** Brandi Vincent
**Date:** February 25, 2026

## Overview

In February 2026, the UAP disclosure landscape shifted significantly when President Donald Trump directed the Pentagon and other agencies to begin identifying and releasing government files related to alien and extraterrestrial life, as well as unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP/UFO). Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth was named as the point person for this initiative, and he publicly doubled down on the commitment, stating the Pentagon would be "in full compliance with that executive order." This article documents the state of play as of late February 2026, including the expanding AARO caseload, institutional challenges, and advocacy reactions.

## Trump's Disclosure Directive

On approximately February 19-20, 2026, President Trump directed Secretary Hegseth and agency leaders to "begin the process of identifying and releasing Government files related to alien and extraterrestrial life" and UAP/UFO information. The directive followed former President Barack Obama's recent podcast comments suggesting that aliens are real, which generated significant public attention and may have influenced the timing of Trump's action.

White House spokeswoman Anna Kelly confirmed the directive, and Hegseth indicated that a formal White House directive would follow to compel increased transparency efforts across the federal government. The DOD was to coordinate with the Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI) on these efforts, suggesting a whole-of-government approach rather than a Pentagon-only initiative.

## Hegseth's Key Statements

Hegseth struck a balance between commitment and caution in his public remarks. He stated the Pentagon is actively working on implementation: "We're digging in." However, he cautioned against overselling timelines, noting it would take "a little time to make sure we assess what this should look like." This measured approach suggests the Pentagon is navigating classification concerns, interagency coordination, and potential national security sensitivities while attempting to meet the president's stated goal of transparency.

## AARO Caseload Expansion

The All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) now examines over 2,000 UAP cases, representing substantial growth of approximately 400 cases since late 2024, when the total stood at roughly 1,600. This increase reflects both the ongoing submission of new reports and the ingestion of historical cases into AARO's tracking systems.

However, the quality and analyzability of these cases varies enormously. Pentagon spokesperson Sue Gough noted that approximately 1,000 reports -- fully half of the total caseload -- "lack sufficient data for analysis" and remain in AARO's Active Archive. This means that only about half of all reported UAP cases contain enough information (sensor data, corroborating witnesses, imagery) to support meaningful investigation.

## Institutional Challenges and Gaps

Several institutional problems were highlighted in the article:

- **Missing Reports:** AARO has not published its 2025 annual report, which was statutorily required. The second volume of UAP historical reports also remains unreleased. These omissions represent failures to meet congressional mandates.
- **Operational Silence:** Officials have been "notably quiet about ongoing projects for over a year," contributing to suspicion among transparency advocates and lawmakers that the office may be withholding or slow-rolling information.
- **Staffing Gaps:** The deputy director position at AARO remains vacant, raising questions about the office's capacity to handle its growing workload and mandate.
- **Official Position on ET:** AARO insiders continue to maintain that no evidence of extraterrestrial involvement has been discovered in any investigated case, a position that frustrates some members of Congress and outside advocates who believe the office's scope or access may be insufficient.

## Transparency Advocacy Reaction

Jordan Flowers, executive director of the Disclosure Foundation, expressed cautious optimism about Hegseth's comments but criticized AARO's failure to meet its statutory obligations. Flowers urged expedited release of unclassified videos and photos, citing government records potentially dating back 80 years that pose "no conceivable threat to national security." His comments reflect a broader frustration in the UAP transparency community that even clearly unclassified historical materials have not been proactively released.
