# We Didn't Find Answers in 2025, but UFO Researchers Say the Search Continues
**Source:** https://www.space.com/space-exploration/search-for-life/we-didnt-find-answers-in-2025-but-ufo-researchers-say-the-search-continues
**Author:** Space.com staff
**Date:** Late 2025

## Overview

This Space.com article provides a candid assessment of the state of UFO/UAP research at the close of 2025. While acknowledging that no definitive answers were found during the year, it documents significant institutional progress, identifies key barriers to advancement, and profiles the major researchers and organizations driving the field forward. The article's tone is notably sober compared to more sensational coverage, reflecting Space.com's science journalism orientation.

## Central Finding: No Definitive Answers, But Significant Progress

The article's headline captures its thesis: despite a year of unprecedented institutional attention, scientific instrumentation development, and government action, UFO researchers did not produce definitive proof of what UAP phenomena are. However, the infrastructure for eventually answering that question advanced substantially. The article treats this as normal scientific progress rather than failure, noting that complex phenomena require sustained investigation.

## Key Researchers and Their Perspectives

### Michael Cifone -- Society for UAP Studies

Michael Cifone, affiliated with the Society for UAP Studies, emphasized that serious scientists are now engaging with UAP using proper instrumentation and methodology. He offered a measured assessment: "Science doesn't always go as planned. In any case, there's a lot of work to be done." This framing positions UAP research as an emerging scientific discipline rather than a quest for dramatic revelations, and acknowledges the inherent uncertainty and slow pace of rigorous investigation.

### Robert Powell -- Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies (SCU)

Robert Powell of the Scientific Coalition for UAP Studies highlighted a critical bottleneck: detecting the extreme acceleration signatures reported in UAP encounters requires expensive, high-precision equipment that civilian researchers currently lack. His specific estimates were revealing:

- **$10-100 million** would be needed to deploy a network of **930 automated camera systems** across the United States
- Military sensor systems that could provide relevant data remain **unavailable to civilian researchers**
- The gap between what military sensors can detect and what civilian scientists can access represents a fundamental barrier to progress

Powell's testimony underscores the tension between government-held data and the civilian scientific community's ability to independently investigate UAP claims.

### Ryan Graves -- AIAA UAP Integration Committee

Ryan Graves, a former U.S. Navy F/A-18 pilot who has become one of the most prominent UAP advocates, chairs the AIAA Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena Integration Committee. Graves stated: "Highly credible people and professional observers are seeing objects" that display unexplained capabilities. He emphasized that regardless of what the objects are, anything operating in sovereign airspace without identification represents a potential **national security threat**.

Graves' perspective is notable because it sidesteps the question of extraterrestrial origin entirely, focusing instead on the practical implications for airspace safety and national defense. This reframing has been strategically effective in generating bipartisan political support for UAP investigation.

## Institutional Progress in 2025

### University of Wurzburg -- AllSkyCAM Systems

The University of Wurzburg in Germany began developing "AllSkyCAM" systems in collaboration with Germany's civil aviation authority. These automated camera systems are designed to continuously monitor the sky and flag anomalous objects for analysis. This represents a significant international development, as it marks a European academic institution committing resources to UAP detection infrastructure.

### Galileo Project -- Harvard University

The Galileo Project, based at Harvard University, continued designing sensor arrays intended to detect anomalies potentially of non-terrestrial origin. Founded by astronomer Avi Loeb, the project represents the highest-profile academic UAP research initiative in the United States. The project's sensor arrays are designed to collect multi-spectral data that could characterize UAP in ways that eyewitness testimony and single-sensor military footage cannot.

### AIAA Standards Development

The American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics (AIAA) continued establishing reporting standards and peer-reviewed research protocols for UAP investigation. As the world's largest aerospace technical society, AIAA's involvement lends significant professional credibility to the field and provides a framework for standardized data collection and analysis.

### Department of Defense AARO

The Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) continued its investigation efforts throughout 2025, though the article notes ongoing tension between AARO's public findings (which have not confirmed non-human origin for any UAP case) and whistleblower claims of legacy retrieval programs that allegedly operate outside AARO's purview.

## Primary Barriers to Progress

The article identifies three major obstacles to advancing UAP research:

1. **Funding limitations**: Civilian research efforts operate on budgets far below what would be needed for comprehensive sensor coverage. Powell's $10-100 million estimate for a U.S. camera network illustrates the scale of investment required.

2. **Historical stigma**: Despite progress, the "UFO stigma" continues to deter some scientists and institutions from engaging with the topic. Career risk remains a factor, particularly for early-career researchers.

3. **Military sensor access**: The most capable sensors for detecting and characterizing UAP are operated by the U.S. military and intelligence community. Civilian researchers cannot access this data, creating a knowledge asymmetry that prevents independent verification of government claims.

## Broader Context

The article situates 2025 within a multi-year arc that began with the 2017 New York Times revelations about the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) and accelerated through David Grusch's 2023 congressional testimony. While each year has brought new institutional developments, the fundamental question -- what are these objects? -- remains unanswered.

The article implicitly suggests that answering this question may require years or decades of sustained scientific effort, and that the most important development of 2025 was not any single sighting or revelation, but the construction of institutional infrastructure that could eventually produce answers.
