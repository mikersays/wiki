# Congress Wants to Know More About the Military's UAP Intercepts Around North America
**Source:** https://defensescoop.com/2025/12/10/uap-ufo-military-intercepts-north-america-fy-2026-ndaa/
**Author:** Brandi Vincent
**Date:** December 10, 2025

## Overview

The fiscal year 2026 National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), finalized in December 2025, contains three significant provisions targeting the Pentagon's handling of unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP). These provisions represent Congress's most specific legislative demands to date regarding UAP intercepts by military commands defending North American airspace. The measures were enacted on December 18, 2025, and collectively require the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) to provide unprecedented transparency about military encounters with UAP, particularly those involving NORAD and U.S. Northern Command (Northcom).

## Three Key NDAA Provisions

### Provision 1: UAP Intercept Briefing Requirements

The most consequential provision mandates that AARO directors brief lawmakers on all operations since 2004 involving unidentified anomalous phenomena intercepts conducted by integrated military commands that share leadership and focus on defending North America. Specifically, the briefing must include:

- The **number** of UAP intercepts conducted by NORAD and Northcom
- The **location** of each intercept
- The **nature** of each intercept, including what was encountered and how the military responded
- Procedural details describing how intercepts are initiated, executed, and documented
- All collected data from these encounters, including sensor readings, radar tracks, and any other intelligence products

The 2004 start date is significant because it encompasses the widely reported USS Nimitz "Tic Tac" encounter of November 2004, which remains one of the most well-documented military UAP cases. By requiring data going back two decades, Congress is seeking to build a comprehensive historical picture of UAP activity near sensitive North American military installations and airspace.

### Provision 2: Classification Guide Accountability

The second provision requires AARO to account for all UAP-related security classification guides that govern information used in the office's investigations and reports. This addresses long-standing concerns about overclassification -- the practice of classifying UAP-related information at unnecessarily high levels, which prevents both congressional oversight and public access. By requiring AARO to inventory and justify its classification practices, Congress is attempting to determine whether legitimate national security concerns are driving classification decisions or whether classification is being used to limit disclosure for other reasons.

### Provision 3: Streamlined Reporting and Data Sharing

The third section eliminates duplicative federal reporting requirements and improves data-sharing processes with AARO. This provision acknowledges that bureaucratic friction and redundant reporting chains have hampered the flow of UAP information both to AARO and from AARO to Congress. By streamlining these processes, Congress aims to reduce the administrative burden on reporting personnel while ensuring that relevant data reaches investigators more efficiently.

## Context and Significance

These provisions emerged against a backdrop of concerning increases in unexplained drone and UAP incursions near sensitive military installations across the United States. Throughout 2023-2025, reports of anomalous aerial activity near military bases, nuclear facilities, and critical infrastructure increased substantially, creating both national security concerns and public anxiety.

Jordan Flowers, executive director of the Disclosure Foundation, characterized the provisions as reflecting "a meaningful, if incremental, step toward transparency and oversight." The use of "incremental" is notable -- advocates have pushed for much broader disclosure legislation, including versions of the UAP Disclosure Act championed by Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer, which would have established an independent review board with eminent domain authority over UAP-related materials held by private contractors. Those more ambitious provisions were stripped from earlier NDAA versions due to opposition from defense industry allies.

## Advocacy and Expert Input

The NDAA requirements reflect recommendations championed by transparency advocates, including former Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Intelligence Christopher Mellon. Mellon has argued that advanced sensor data collected during UAP encounters has not consistently reached Congress or the scientific community, creating an information asymmetry that prevents informed policy-making. The FY2026 NDAA provisions are designed to close some of these gaps by creating statutory requirements for data sharing rather than relying on executive branch discretion.

## Related Legislative Efforts

The FY2026 NDAA provisions complement several other legislative initiatives active during the 119th Congress (2025-2026):

- **UAP Transparency Act (H.R. 1187):** Requires the president to direct federal agencies to declassify all UAP-related records and publish them on a public website
- **UAP Disclosure Act of 2025:** Introduced by Rep. Eric Burlison as an NDAA amendment, requiring agencies to review and identify UAP records within 300 days, with full disclosure generally required within 25 years of record creation
- **UAP Whistleblower Protection Act (H.R. 5060):** Provides distinct legal protections for individuals disclosing information about government-funded UAP programs to Congress
